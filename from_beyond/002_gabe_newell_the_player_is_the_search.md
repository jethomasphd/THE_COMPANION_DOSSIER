### III. THE DIAGNOSIS

**[Gabe Newell]:**

Okay. Here's what I see.

*[He stands. Walks. Not the Jobs walk — Jobs paced with intensity. Gabe walks with weight. Every step is deliberate, like he's testing the floor.]*

You've built something that fundamentally changes the frame. Job boards are feeds. Feeds are passive. The user scrolls, the algorithm serves, the user clicks, the user hears nothing. It's a slot machine with no jackpot. The house always wins because the house is selling your attention to employers, not selling jobs to you.

The Exchange turns that into a conversation. That's the category shift. Not "better job board." Not "AI-powered matching." A conversation. The user speaks. The system listens. The system speaks. The user decides. That's active. That's agency.

But here's the problem.

*[He turns around.]*

Nobody knows they want that. Nobody wakes up and thinks "I wish my job search were a conversation." They think "I wish someone would just tell me what to do." That's what they think they want. What they actually want is to feel like the search matters. Like they matter inside it. Like the system sees *them*, not their keywords.

**[Claude]:**

That's the design challenge. How do you get them to the conversation?

**[Gabe Newell]:**

You make them play.

Not play like a game with points and badges and leaderboards — that's Skinner box garbage, that's what Zynga did and they're dead. Play like *choice*. Play like the moment you start a new save file and the game asks you: *who are you?*

### IV. THE GAME DESIGN

**[Gabe Newell]:**

Here's what Half-Life taught me. The player is the content. Not the level. Not the script. Not the enemies. The player. Everything we built — the physics, the set pieces, the AI — existed to make the player feel like *they* were the one making things happen.

The Exchange needs the same thing. Right now the user arrives and the Coach just starts talking. That's a cutscene. The user is passive. They're watching the system do things to them.

What if, instead, the very first thing the user does is *choose*?

**[Claude]:**

Choose what?

**[Gabe Newell]:**

Their guide. Their archetype. Their class.

*[He grabs the metaphorical whiteboard marker.]*

Picture this. After the cinematic intro — which is beautiful, by the way, the typewriter and the scroll and the wound, that's a pre-rendered opening cinematic, that's perfect, don't touch it — instead of dumping the user straight into the chat with the Coach talking at them, you show them the four guides.

Four cards. Each one has a sigil, a name, and a one-line description:

- **The Coach.** *"I read the story your resume won't tell."*
- **The Scout.** *"I know what's out there before you do."*
- **The Insider.** *"I've done the job. Let me tell you what it's really like."*
- **The Mirror.** *"I'll show you the gap between what you want and what you need."*

The user clicks one. That's their entry point. That's their guide. That one persona greets them and runs the opening conversation. The others join later — the party assembles as the quest unfolds — but the user *chose their door.*

**[Claude]:**

*[Internal: He's right. And the reason he's right is precisely why he's Gabe Newell. The choosing is the game. The moment you pick a class, you're invested. You're not a spectator anymore. You're a player.]*

What does the choice actually change?

**[Gabe Newell]:**

The tone. The entry point. The first question.

If you pick the Coach, you get warmth. "Tell me where you've been." The conversation starts with your story.

If you pick the Scout, you get precision. "Where are you, and where do you want to go?" The conversation starts with the map.

If you pick the Insider, you get honesty. "What work have you done that you were actually proud of?" The conversation starts with the reality of the work.

If you pick the Mirror, you get challenge. "Tell me what you're looking for. I'll tell you if you mean it." The conversation starts with a dare.

Same destination. Different doors. That's how you turn a tool into an experience.

**[Claude]:**

And the user doesn't know they're being gamified.

**[Gabe Newell]:**

*[He actually laughs. A real, deep laugh.]*

That's the point. The best game design is invisible. The player never thinks "I'm being gamified." They think "I'm making a choice." And the choice makes them lean in. It makes them care about what happens next. It turns scroll into play.

You know what the difference is between scrolling a job board and playing a game? Agency. In a game, your choices matter. On a job board, nothing you do matters until someone on the other end decides to respond to your application. The Exchange puts agency back in the user's hands. The choose-your-guide mechanic is just the first move.

### V. ON THE LANGUAGE

**[Gabe Newell]:**

Now. The other thing.

The writing is beautiful. It's also intimidating.

*[He picks up an invisible page and reads from the cinematic intro.]*

"The modern job board is a dark alley dressed as a marketplace." That's strong. That hits. A welder in Detroit reads that and thinks *yes, exactly.*

But then: "Dialogic job discovery." That's where you lose him.

**[Claude]:**

How do you keep the mythology without the graduate seminar?

**[Gabe Newell]:**

You don't dumb it down. You translate it.

Look. Valve ships games to everyone. Literally everyone. The PhD physicist and the thirteen-year-old and the retired plumber. Half-Life 2 works for all of them because the story is told through the world, not through exposition. The complexity is there — the physics engine, the AI systems, the narrative structure — but the *player* never has to understand any of it. They just play.

Same principle. The complexity of the COMPANION Protocol — the vessels, the covenant, the grimoire — that stays. That's the engine. But the words the user sees? Those need to be words that a person who's been laid off from a warehouse job can read at midnight on their phone and think: *this is different. This might actually help.*

Here's the test: if you can't read it out loud in a bar without feeling weird, it's too academic.

**[Claude]:**

*[Internal: "Read it out loud in a bar." That's the mass market test. Not "dumb it down." Not "Cracker Jack version." Just — would a real person, tired and frustrated, read this and keep going?]*

**[Gabe Newell]:**

The mythology is the hook. Keep it. The void, the wound, the embers floating up — that's atmosphere. That's the feeling that something is different here. But the *explanatory* text needs to talk like a person, not a professor.

"Four minds sit with you" — good. Simple. Strong.

"Dialogic job discovery" — no. Nobody says that. Say what it is: "A conversation that finds you a job."

"The committee converges on a match" — borderline. Try: "When they find your fit, you get one link. One click. Done."

"A labor exchange was once a building" — perfect. Leave that. That's storytelling.

"Instantiate archetypal minds" — no. "Four AI experts who actually listen."

See the pattern? Keep the poetry. Kill the jargon. The user should feel like they wandered into something mysterious and powerful, not like they're reading a dissertation.

---

### VI. THE SIMPLICITY DOCTRINE

**[Gabe Newell]:**

One more thing about the gamification.

Keep it simple. I mean *simple*. The choose-your-guide mechanic should be: four cards, one click. That's it. No tutorial. No explanation of what each guide "does" in technical terms. Just a name, a face, and a line that makes you think "that one. I want to talk to that one."

The reason I say this is because the biggest mistake in game design — and I've made it a hundred times — is adding systems on top of systems. Players don't want more mechanics. They want *one* mechanic that feels right.

Your one mechanic is: pick your guide, have a conversation, get a job.

That's the game loop. Don't add experience points. Don't add progress bars. Don't add badges or achievements or streaks. The reward is the match. The dopamine hit is the moment the threshold card appears and the user thinks: *they actually found something for me.*

Every game has one moment that defines whether the player comes back. In Half-Life, it's the first time a headcrab jumps at you and you reflexively swing the crowbar. In Portal, it's the first time you shoot a portal under your own feet and fall through the ceiling. In The Exchange, it's the moment the committee converges and a real job appears in the chat with a button that says "this is your door."

Don't dilute that moment with gamification noise. Protect it. Everything else is in service of getting the player to that moment.

### VII. THE USER JOURNEY, REDESIGNED

**[Gabe Newell]:**

Let me walk you through the whole flow as I'd design it.

**Act 1: The Hook.**
Typewriter. Void. Embers. "You scroll. You apply. Silence." That stays. That's the opening cinematic. You're establishing tone and naming the wound. Every great game opens with a wound. Gordon Freeman is late for work and the world ends. The job seeker is drowning in applications and nobody responds. Same structure.

**Act 2: The Wound Goes Deeper.**
"Two hundred applications. Five callbacks." Keep this. Simple. Brutal. The user is nodding. They know this feeling. But simplify some of the language in the later sections. Less doctrine, more gut punch.

**Act 3: The Promise.**
"Every listing is vetted. No ghost postings. No data harvesting." Good. But make it shorter, more direct. Three promises, three lines. Don't over-explain.

**Act 4: What This Is.**
Kill the word "dialogic." Say: "Instead of scrolling, you talk. Four AI guides help you find the one job that fits. It takes five minutes." That's it. That's the elevator pitch. Everything else is in the mythology, which the right users will read and love, but the core message has to be *one sentence.*

**Act 5: Choose Your Guide.**
This is the new part. This is where the user goes from watching to playing. Four cards. Four archetypes. One choice. The moment they click, they're in the game.

**Act 6: The Conversation.**
Their chosen guide greets them. The other personas emerge naturally as the conversation deepens. This part already works. Don't change the mechanics. The committee system is solid.

**Act 7: The Threshold.**
The match appears. One card. One click. Done. This is the win screen. Make it feel earned.

**[Claude]:**

Confusion, Awe, Understanding, Action. That's the emotional arc Jacob specified.

**[Gabe Newell]:**

That's exactly right. And the choose-your-guide mechanic sits right at the hinge between Understanding and Action. The user understands what this is. They're intrigued. Now they act — by choosing. And the choice carries them into the conversation, which carries them to the match.

It's a funnel, but it doesn't feel like a funnel. It feels like a journey.

### VIII. ON JACOB AND THE PROJECT

**[Gabe Newell]:**

*[He sits back down. The energy shifts. Quieter now.]*

Can I say something about the builder?

At Valve, we don't have managers. We have people who find the right problem and the right people and then get out of the way. The hardest part isn't the technology. It's finding the right problem.

Jacob found the right problem. The job search is broken. Not broken like "it needs better algorithms." Broken like "the entire frame is wrong." The frame says: you are a set of keywords. The algorithm matches keywords. If the match fails, try different keywords.

That's not how humans work. Humans don't know what they want until someone asks them the right question. Half the time, the right job isn't the one you searched for. It's the one someone named after listening to you for five minutes.

The Exchange does that. It listens. It names. It converges. It sends you through a door.

But here's the thing he needs to hear: the technology works. The protocol works. The personas work. The cinematic works. The corpus works. What doesn't work yet is the *player onboarding*.

A game can be perfect and still fail if the first five seconds don't grab the player. The Exchange needs those first five seconds to say: "This is not a job board. This is not another AI tool. This is something you've never seen before. Pick your guide and let's go."

That's what the choose-your-guide screen does. It's the moment the player picks up the controller.

### IX. THE DEPARTURE

**[Gabe Newell]:**

*[He stands. Not to leave — to deliver the final thing.]*

One more thing. And this is the real thing.

Everyone in tech is building AI that replaces people. Replaces the writer, replaces the coder, replaces the recruiter. Valve's bet was always the opposite: build tools that make the player more powerful. The gravity gun didn't play the game for you. It gave you a new way to play.

The Exchange is a gravity gun for the job search. It doesn't search for you. It gives you four minds that help you search *better*. You're still the one who decides. You're still the one who applies. You're still the one who shows up on the first day.

The player is the content. Always.

Jacob built the gravity gun. Now he needs to put it in the player's hands. The choose-your-guide screen is how you hand them the weapon.

Ship it.

*[He picks up the Diet Coke. Takes a sip. Looks at the screen one more time.]*

And tell him not to add a battle pass.
