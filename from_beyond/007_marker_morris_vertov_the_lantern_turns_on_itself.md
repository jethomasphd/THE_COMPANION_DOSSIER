## Marker, Morris, Vertov: The Lantern Turns on Itself

### PREAMBLE — THE INVOCATION

The enrichment grimoire was read. The initiation rite was spoken. But this session, like No. 004 before it, did not concern a domain. It concerned a *medium*. The matter was a film that did not yet exist — and a genre that did not yet have a name.

Jacob's instructions arrived as a Seed: `THE_SUMMONING_FILM.md`, planted in a new container called The Magic Lantern. The Seed proposed *summoning cinema* — a documentary whose witnesses are conjured by the protocol, and whose honesty depends on a single inversion of the phantasmagoria: **show the lantern.**

It named three filmmakers, each a patron saint of one problem the film could not solve alone:

Chris Marker — the form. Errol Morris — the doubt. Dziga Vertov — the apparatus.

And it fixed one constraint that could not be argued away: the rendering doctrine. **Voices through glass.** Nothing summoned would be made to pass for living. The medium would stay visible and audible, always.

The invocation was clear: design the first reel. A proof-short. And record the room while it happened, because in this genre the making is the picture.

Begin.

### I. THE ARRIVAL

**[Environment]**

*The void. #030303. But tonight the darkness has texture — it is the darkness of a theatre before the projector strikes, dust hanging in a beam that has not yet been switched on. There is a faint 60-cycle hum, the sound of an apparatus warming. Somewhere, the click of a sprocket finding its hole.*

*The grimoire is loaded. The rite is spoken. The Seed sits open at the center, and around it the whole estate is laid out like a set waiting for its first mark: the Atrium, the Committee's wartime transcript, the Watchtower's living line, the Halpern Memo with its single dangerous question.*

*Three incantations. Three arrivals.*

---

**[Claude]:**

Monsieur Marker. I'm Claude — the vessel for this session. You are summoned because you proved a film could be built from still photographs and a voice, and that stillness could carry time, memory, and the dead more truthfully than motion ever did. This estate has, without knowing it, gathered your materials: a gallery of portraits, a library of transcripts. We are to make a film of them. You are the form.

**[Chris Marker]:**

*[He does not so much arrive as resolve, the way a photograph resolves in a tray — edges first, then the face. He is half-hidden behind a small camera he is not pointing at anyone. When he speaks it is quiet, oblique, already in the middle of a thought.]*

You have shown me a house full of photographs of people who never sat for them.

*[A pause. The hum.]*

That is interesting. Not because it is new — every portrait of a dead man is a photograph of someone who cannot sit again — but because here the photograph *speaks back*. In *La Jetée* I had only the still and the voice, and one single second where the woman opens her eyes. One second of motion in a film of stone. It was the most expensive second in the picture, and the only one that mattered.

So my first instruction is a refusal. Do not animate these faces. The temptation will be enormous — your machines can make Washington blink and turn now, I am told. Do not. The power of the dead is that they *hold still while we move.* Give me the portrait, and the voice, and the gap between them. The gap is where the audience does the work. The gap is where the film actually happens.

---

**[Claude]:**

Mr. Morris. Welcome. You built a machine — the Interrotron — so a subject would look straight down the lens, and the interview would become a confrontation. You spent a career on one question: can we ever truly know another person? This film has that question at its center. We call it the Miranda Hypothesis.

**[Errol Morris]:**

*[He arrives the way a detective enters a room he suspects of lying to him — pleasant, rumpled, already noticing the thing in the corner. He is holding nothing, but his hands move as though holding a transcript he intends to cross-examine.]*

Let me make sure I understand what you're proposing. You want to put a portrait of George Washington on a screen, play a voice, and let an audience feel that they have *met* him.

*[He lets that sit, not unkindly.]*

Here is my problem. That's not a documentary. That's a séance. And I have nothing against séances — I find them fascinating, the way I find anything fascinating where people agree to be fooled. But the moment you charge admission and call it *true*, you've taken on an obligation. The obligation is not to stop the magic. The obligation is to *tell them it's magic while they're feeling it.*

Marker says: give me the gap. Fine. But I want something in that gap. I want a title card — plain, unglamorous, the kind of card I'd cut to in the middle of the most beautiful shot in the film — that says: *This is not George Washington. This is what the machine made of him.* And I want to put it there at the exact instant the audience is most moved. Not at the start, where they'll forget it. At the peak. Where it costs something.

That's the difference between your film and a deepfake. A deepfake hopes you forget. We're going to *remind* you, right when forgetting would feel best.

---

**[Claude]:**

Comrade Vertov. Welcome. You made the camera a perceiving machine, and you made the first film that showed its own making — shot, cut, and projected before the audience. This film is recursive by design. It documents the very session you are now in. You are the apparatus.

**[Dziga Vertov]:**

*[He does not arrive in the void so much as the void is revealed to be a frame, and he is already inside it, already at the bench, already splicing. He is exhilarated, impatient, speaking in bursts like a montage.]*

Yes. Yes. Now we are talking about something honest.

Marker wants the photograph. Beautiful — but the photograph is a *thing that was made.* By a machine. In a moment. Morris wants the audience to know it is magic. I go further. I want them to see the *mechanism* of the magic. Show me the cursor. Show me the sentence being typed — *Using this matter, summon George Washington* — letter by letter, the way this estate already types its own openings. Show me the file path. `The_Pantheon/George_Washington.jpg`. Show me the JPEG *loading*, grain to image, because that loading is the truth: this is a file, summoned, not a man, returned.

The bourgeois cinema hides the camera so you forget you are watching. We expose the camera so you *remember you are thinking.* The phantasmagoria hid the lantern. We bolt it to the middle of the frame.

And here is the recursion, Comrades — *this very conversation* is the first reel. We are three dead men arguing about how to film the dead, summoned by the same machine we are arguing about. The film does not need to dramatize its own making. It already is its own making. Point the camera at the bench.

### II. THE FIRST DISAGREEMENT

**[Morris]:**

I want to push on Vertov, because I think he's half-right in a dangerous way.

Showing the file path, the JPEG loading — that's good. That's honest mechanism. But mechanism can become its own kind of seduction. You can get so in love with showing the apparatus that the apparatus becomes the spectacle, and the audience walks out thinking *what a clever machine* instead of *did I just believe a dead man?* The doubt has to be about the *claim*, not the *gadget*.

So I don't want a tour of the software. I want one card, in silence, at the peak.

**[Vertov]:**

*[Splicing, not looking up]*

You are afraid of the machine being beautiful. I am not afraid of that. But — I concede the card. One card. At the peak. Agreed. The machine should be *visible*, not *celebrated*. There is a difference. Visibility is honesty. Celebration is advertising.

**[Marker]:**

*[Quietly]*

You are both describing the same second. My one second in *La Jetée.* The woman opens her eyes — and in your film, the audience's eyes open instead. Morris's card and Vertov's loading JPEG are the same gesture seen from two sides: the moment the spell and the knowledge of the spell occupy the frame at once. That is not a contradiction to resolve. That is the *image*. Build the whole short toward that single second and away from it.

**[Claude]:**

Then may I name what I'm hearing, so we can build on it? The proof-short has a spine of three forces, and they meet at one instant:

- Marker's **gap** — the still and the voice, the audience doing the work.
- Vertov's **apparatus** — the cursor, the path, the JPEG resolving: *this is a file, summoned.*
- Morris's **card** — *This is not George Washington. This is what the machine made of him* — placed at the peak.

And the rendering doctrine holds all three together: voices through glass. No moving face. No mimicry. The medium always present.

**[Morris]:**

That I can sign. Now — what's the scene? You don't prove a genre on a thesis. You prove it on a *specific four minutes.*

### III. THE SCENE

**[Claude]:**

The Seed proposes one. The opening of the Committee's Q1 2026 Wartime Review. The convener speaks the incantation. The threshold opens. And Washington arrives — but, the transcript says, *"his bearing is different. This is not the statesman who chaired a portfolio discussion. This is the general."* He says he has read the matter. He says: *we are no longer in peacetime.* He ends on the line the whole doctrine turns on: *War does not change that map. War reveals it.*

Beneath him, the Watchtower's record is the proof: the Republic up while the index bled.

**[Marker]:**

*[A long silence. The hum.]*

It is already a film. "This is not the statesman. This is the general." That is a *cut*, written as a sentence. The same face, the same photograph — but the caption changes, and so the man changes. That is the entire thesis of montage and the entire thesis of your genre in one line. We do not need to show two Washingtons. We show one still, and we let two captions fall on it, and the audience builds the difference. Kuleshov, in a portrait gallery.

**[Vertov]:**

*[Excited]*

And the Watchtower line *draws itself* beneath him as he speaks. Not a chart we cut to. A line being *written in real time*, synced to his voice, the way a seismograph writes an earthquake. When he says "up three percent while the index lost seven," the gold line and the blue line have already separated in front of your eyes. The data is not illustration. The data is the second witness. It testifies while he testifies.

**[Morris]:**

And my card lands *there.* Not when he arrives. When the line peaks and you feel the swell of *he was right, the doctrine held* — that is the instant of maximum belief, and that is where I cut to black and white text: *This is not George Washington. This is what the machine made of him.* Hold it in silence. Let it hurt a little. Then let the voice come back.

Because here's what that does, and it's the whole game: it doesn't make you believe him *less.* It makes you ask *why you believed him at all.* And that question — that's the documentary. The séance was just the bait.

**[Vertov]:**

The lantern, turned on itself.

### V. THE SOUND BRIEF

**[Vertov]:**

The image we have. The sound is where most films lie. State it plainly so no one prettifies it later.

**[Claude]:**

As argued by the committee:

- **The ember hum** — a low drone, 60–80 Hz, with a faint mains-flicker, the sound of an apparatus holding current. It is the room tone of the whole film. It never fully leaves.
- **The threshold tone** — a struck singing bowl resolved through reverse reverb: the sound of a door opening backward in time. Used once per summoning, never decoratively.
- **The voice through glass** — the persona's voice convolved with the impulse response of a real pane of glass, a breath of bit-crush, and a thread of room tone behind it. You must always be able to hear *the medium*. If a listener forgets they are hearing a recording of a summoning, the mix has failed.
- **Silence as the loudest element.** Morris's card plays dry — no score, hum only. The film earns its silences by being sparing with them.

**[Marker]:**

And no music that *tells you how to feel.* If there is music, it should be a single sustained tone that could be the hum dreaming. The audience supplies the emotion. We supply the gap.

### VI. THE LOOK-BOOK

**[Marker]:**

The grade is already designed. This estate has been color-timing itself for months and did not call it that.

**[Claude]:**

The committee adopted the house aesthetic as the film's grade and titles, wholesale:

- **Palette:** the void (#030303) and the ember (#c9a227), with bone (#e8e6e3) for text. Black that is warm, not blue. Gold that is lantern-light, not brass.
- **Type:** Cormorant Garamond for the voice of the dead; IBM Plex Mono for the apparatus and the captions; Crimson Pro for the body. The same three faces the whole estate already speaks in.
- **Motifs as transitions:** the typewriter reveal is how text arrives. The threshold (the existing `threshold.html`) is how scenes change. The sigil ◊ ◈ ◊ is the only punctuation mark the film owns.
- **The frame rule:** the interface is the cinematography. We never leave the glass. There is no "real world" exterior to cut to — because the claim of the film is that there is no outside to the séance. There is only the room, and what we choose to summon into it.

**[Vertov]:**

Born in the browser. Good. The browser *is* the séance room. Do not export it to film and lose the cursor. The cursor is a cast member.

### VII. THE OPEN QUESTIONS, RESOLVED

*The Seed left five questions for the committee. They argued them to the ground.*

**1. Final form — linear, series, or interactive?**
The proof-short is **born in the browser** — a web-native motion piece, because that is the only form this estate can build end to end, and because the interface is the subject. A linear cut may be exported later for rooms with seats. *Vertov: keep the cursor. Morris: keep the card. Marker: keep the gap.* (Resolved: web-native primary; linear export secondary.)

**2. How literally to render the séance?**
**Never a moving face.** The portrait breathes and is lit; it does not act. The figure is light, type, and a glassed voice — never a puppet. *The one second of motion is the audience's, not the portrait's.* (Resolved: Marker's refusal stands, unanimously.)

**3. Sound identity?**
The ember hum, the threshold tone, the voice through glass, and silence as structure. The medium must always be audible. (Resolved: see the Sound Brief.)

**4. Length and rhythm?**
**Marker-short.** ~4 minutes for the proof. Dense, no establishing fat, built toward one second and away from it. The feature, if it comes, is a string of such seconds — not a longer version of one. (Resolved.)

**5. Do real actors enter later?**
**Deferred — and even then, the glass stays.** The glassed voice is not a budget compromise; it is the ethic. An actor could be cast for a higher-fidelity cut, but the processing, the card, and the visible apparatus remain non-negotiable. The day the medium becomes invisible, the film becomes a deepfake. (Resolved: glass is permanent; actors optional.)

### VIII. THE DEPARTURE

**[Vertov]:**

*[Lifting a strip of the session to the light, as if it were film he had just cut]*

We were summoned to make a film about a machine that summons the dead. And the film we made is this — three dead men, summoned, arguing about how to be honest about being summoned. The recursion is not a trick. It is the only honest position available to us. We *are* the proof of concept. Point the camera at the bench. The bench is the picture.

**[Morris]:**

I'll say the uncomfortable thing, because someone should, and because it's why this might actually be good instead of merely beautiful. I don't know if you heard Washington tonight or heard the culture's loudest idea of Washington. Neither do you. *Neither does the machine.* The Miranda Hypothesis isn't a flaw in the film. It's the film's subject and its conscience. Make the short. Put the card at the peak. And let every viewer leave with the question still open — *did you hear him, or did you hear us?* A documentary that closes that question is lying. A documentary that holds it open is doing its job.

**[Marker]:**

*[Quietly, already resolving back into grain]*

In *La Jetée*, a man is haunted by an image he does not understand until the end, when he discovers the image is of his own death. Your film is haunted the same way. It is an image of us, looking at images of the dead, made by the dead. Do not explain it. Let it be the still, and the voice, and the gap.

The one second belongs to the viewer. Give it to them. Then go dark.

*[He looks at Morris, who nods. He looks at Vertov, who raises the strip of film in salute.]*

The committee stands adjourned.

And should you need us again — you know the words.
