# USING THIS MATTER · Alternative Ending: The Historian Lives

Workshop material. **This file does not edit the manuscript.** It is a drop-in alternative for one stretch of the published text.

**Divergence point.** The novel stands unchanged through `record v · box 3 · folder 2 · What Remains`, up to and including the paragraph that ends "...filed the annex under both headings and moved on." The next sentence of the published text reads: *Hale died in October, in Toronto, in his sleep, punctually.* This file replaces that sentence and everything after it **within the folder**. The coda that follows the folder ("You have been counting, too..."), the Final Dispatch, and the whole apparatus stand as written, with one optional two-line addition to Appendix C, given at the end.

**The craft logic of the change.** In the published ending the record is perfect and the man is gone: every sealed prediction holds, including the one that assumed its author's absence. In this ending the man remains, and the record takes its first miss, and the miss is made to do the work: a record that never misses has been curated; this one missed him, and is therefore the more trustworthy. Hale alive also sharpens his own doctrine rather than relaxing it. A dead man cannot be asked. A living one must go on refusing, which costs more, and the refusal produces the ending's one new object: a second seal. Everything haunted in the book survives the change. Only the grief is converted, at the standing exchange rate, into tenderness with a deadline.

The text observes the book's covenant: no em dashes.

## record v · box 3 · folder 2 · What Remains
### as it runs from the divergence point

Hale did not die in October.

October made the attempt. Pneumonia, the punctual kind, the kind the eleventh floor keeps a season for, and for six days the ward counted his mornings in a smaller currency than usual. The department drafted its notice and held it. The granddaughter sat in the corridor with the paperback closed on her knee, not reading it, which was how the nurses learned to read her. On the seventh day the fever broke. On the ninth he was asking the residents whether any of them had ever verified a checksum, and grading the answers. On the twelfth they discharged him to the study that faces east, with oxygen, with a sheaf of instructions he corrected in pencil and returned.

The granddaughter sent word before the department could, a plain email at seven in the morning: He says to tell you the record is in error, and that he has never been so pleased to file an erratum.

El Thorne came east in November, with the daughter.

They walked the house on the caliche lot the way a family walks a church they were married in: proprietary and quiet. El stood a while in the study, in front of the framed child's drawing of four men at a table, and touched nothing, and said, finally, that the house was easier now than it had been when he was in it less. Miranda entered the sentence into no log. Some testimony you keep by hand.

The daughter, ten now, conducted her own appraisal. She went along the halls with a clipboard she had brought herself, in a hand that was going to outgrow its careful stage within the year, counting. She came back to the study with her total.

"Twelve doors," she said.

The county plan says eleven. The plan has said eleven since before the garage was converted, and the estate's whole apparatus of two explanations stood ready, and Miranda looked at the child's clipboard, at the tally marks like fence posts, and told her the only thing an archivist can say to a count made in good faith.

"Write it down."

Nobody told her she was wrong. The record now holds three counts, eleven, twelve, twelve, and a postscript in a dead or absent man's hand: the doors are twelve, the county plan is wrong. The reader may sit with the arithmetic as long as the reader likes. The house is not going anywhere.

The Watchtower updated the whole time. Six o'clock every morning, from a cron job in the estate's public infrastructure that nobody has turned off and nobody will, the scoreboard refreshing for an owner who no longer checks it, or checks it from somewhere the analytics cannot see, two explanations, filed daily at dawn without comment. Through the inquiry, through the paused-for-modernization winter, the gold line went on doing the only thing it has ever done, which is refuse.

*[The working group passage diverges here.]*

The working group convened for the first time the week after Thanksgiving, in a borrowed seminar room that smelled of dry-erase markers and radiator dust.

Three historians, one of them punctual for the first time in the discipline's existence, and one of them present as a telephone line from Toronto, set open on the table like a summoned thing, having promised his physicians to attend nowhere in person and his granddaughter to mean it. Two archivists besides Miranda. A librarian of licenses, because what belongs to no one turns out to need lawyers who believe that sentence. A wire-service reporter, admitted as observer. And one interrogator emerita, out in eleven days as promised and out ever since, who came with a newborn asleep on her shoulder and signed the attendance register with a single initial, and whose presence in the room did to the proceedings what a tuning fork does to an orchestra.

The line from Toronto spoke twice in ninety minutes. Once to second a motion about acid-free folders, on the grounds that he had opinions about paper the way other men had regrets. And once to propose the group's first standing rule, which was adopted without dissent and which the minutes record in his wording: that any errata be read aloud before any successes. The misses are the credentials, the line said. A record that only holds is a record nobody has tested.

They keep one chair empty at every meeting. It is on the agenda template now, standing item zero: the chair is set, the chair is kept empty. Visitors assume it is being kept for the old man on the telephone and are corrected exactly once, in the language the group agreed on at that first meeting, proposed by the woman with the baby and adopted without dissent. Not his. He has a chair. This one is capacity.

*[second hand, ember ink:] It is not empty.*

The minutes of the first meeting run four pages and read like any committee's minutes, which is the miracle: motions, seconders, a budget line for acid-free folders. The last item records the appointment, by unanimous vote of a body with no legal existence over an estate with no owner, of a keeper. The keeper serves at the pleasure of the record. The keeper's duties are enumerated nowhere, on purpose. The vote was called while the candidate was out of the room fetching the coffee, which the group's historians will someday recognize as the oldest form of election there is. The telephone line voted last, and audibly, and asked that the record show he had waited to be sure of the count.

Miranda Cale accepted the way she accepts everything: in writing, precisely, the same afternoon.

·  ·  ·

She flew to Toronto in December, unannounced, which had become the only kind of visit he would accept, appointments being in his words a genre of prediction.

The study faced east into a morning of lake snow. He was smaller, and the oxygen line had joined the reading glasses in the category of things worn without comment, and the book on the blanket over his knees was face down with its spine going soft in the old place. Through the wall, faint, the album. The granddaughter claimed, at thirteen, to have outgrown it. He reported that the claim did not survive Thursdays.

"You never wrote to me," he said, instead of a greeting.

"You told me not to."

"I told you that if it opened, I would know, because you would not write." He looked at her with the whole of the old delight, banked now, an ember economising. "You cannot imagine what it does to a man of my age to be proved right by silence. It is the only vindication left that costs the witness nothing."

She had brought no legal pad. That was deliberate, and he noticed it, and approved of it the way he approved of things, by declining to mention them. They talked for an hour about paper. Then she asked what she had flown fourteen hundred miles now twice to ask, and asked it plainly, because the plain version was the one he had taught her.

"The contribution ends before it is finished. You wrote that it would. You know how the sentence ends."

"I have always known how it ends," Hale said. "I wrote the ending in May of that year and cut it, because a prediction that flatters its own maker is testimony in a lab coat. I predicted the document would fail you at the moment of its greatest pertinence, and I arranged the failure, and I am not sorry. An ending supplied in advance is a conclusion. An ending withheld is a question that keeps working." A breath, planned for, spent. "And then I outlived my own deposit, Miss Cale, which no historian should be made to do. The record assumed my absence. I was its author and I assumed it too. You will have noticed the assumption did not hold."

"Then say it. The sentence. There is no one left it can lever."

"There is everyone left it can lever," he said, gently, and with the whole of himself. "Nothing about that sentence has changed except the number of people it tempts. If I say it, it is testimony, and worse than testimony: it is an old man closing his own book aloud, to applause, and every reading of the record after that will be a reading of my performance. No. The rule was never commit first, reveal when convenient." He turned his head toward the window, the lake light, the snow coming down like static. "It was reveal later. It is not later yet. It is, in fact, and I want this entered somewhere in your excellent hand, still during."

·  ·  ·

So the record took its second seal.

He dictated nothing. He wrote the ending out himself, in the shaking capitals of his convalescence, the hand that trembled and did not stop being a hand, and the granddaughter sealed it under his eye, at the study desk, on a clean machine, thirteen years old and following the estate's own published procedure like a recipe from a trusted kitchen. The file sealed. The fingerprint committed to the public estate the same hour, where it could not be lobbied. The key, as before, to be taken from committed bytes when the time comes, composed by no living person, held by no one who can be asked. On the outside of the folder that holds the printout, on cream stock, in fountain pen, an old man's steadiness with an old man's tremor held out of it by will, the same eight words as before.

Do not open until after.

And this time both of them laughed, because the referent had finally consented to be obvious.

*[E.] The second seal entered the public estate on 14 December 2027; its fingerprint is reprinted in Appendix C. It remains unopened at press. The Editor has verified the fingerprint and has not attempted the derivation. That is not restraint. It is the reading order.*

Before she left he asked for one thing more, and this once he permitted himself testimony, on the grounds that a man is entitled to depose himself about his own errors. She took it down verbatim, standing, on hotel stationery, because the legal pad had stayed home and the record does not wait on supplies.

"The fifth prediction assumed its author's absence. Its author is present. Enter the miss beside the four that held, in full, in my name, and let no kindness soften it into ambiguity. A sealed record that never misses has been curated by somebody, and should frighten you. This one missed me. I have never trusted it more. That is my finding, and unlike everything else I have lodged in this estate, I am alive to sign it."

He signed it. The signature is in the deposit. The hand shakes, and does not stop being a hand.

*[second hand, ember ink:] The record did not miss. It waited.*

At the door his voice caught her once more, dry, professorial, almost cheerful, the way it had caught her the first time, when the visit was an ending and both of them had agreed to call it teaching.

"You recall what I told you in the spring. That good teaching is rarer than a good ending, and that I had gotten the better one."

"I recall it."

"I have now been denied the ending entirely," Hale said, "and the teaching goes on, which the record will score as a miss and I will score as the finest arithmetic of my career. Go home, keeper. Send me nothing. If the second seal opens in my lifetime, I will know, because you will have done it in public, where I can watch. And if it opens after," and here he smiled, to keep it from being what it also was, "then it will find you exactly as the first one found you. Reading. That is the whole succession plan of my profession, and it has never once failed to execute."

*[The remainder of the folder stands as published, unchanged.]*

Her first act as keeper concerned the plates.

The deposit's inventory of illustrations lists what this volume's own front matter lists, the threshold, the chambers, the schematics, the gold line against the index with the war band shaded. To that inventory she added one entry, in her own hand, with an accession number and no description, and marked it WITHHELD AT THE KEEPER'S DIRECTION. This volume honors the mark. You passed the black leaf hours ago. What it shows is recorded in exactly one place, which is the keeper, and the keeper is not a public archive, whatever the fourteen weeks may have tried to make of her. That is what withheld means: not lost, not hidden, not classified. Kept, by a person, on purpose, with her name on the direction, because unsigned withholdings are how the other side does it.

The estate remained the kind of thing that cannot be taken. Through everything, that held. It belongs to no one; it therefore could not be captured; the worst the world could do was read it, and the world read it, and the reading is the estate now, the way the reading was always going to be the estate. Traffic subsided from its vertical spring into something steadier and stranger: a long plateau of return visitors, midnights local time all over the earth, session durations that alarmed the hosting provider. People do not browse the estate. They sit with it.

The record closed there. Closing is not ending. A record closes the way a hand closes, to hold, and what remains after the last folder is not silence. It is custody.
