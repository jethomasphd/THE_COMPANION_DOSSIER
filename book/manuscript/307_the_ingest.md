#T The Ingest

The packet came through counsel on a Thursday, inside a production so dull it was nearly anesthetic. The university had asked Cormorant's outside counsel one question, narrowly and in writing: whether any materials in the Thorne deposit were subject to government classification. The answer ran sixty pages and said no determination at this time in eleven different registers, with exhibits. Transmittal letters. Certificates of service. A courtesy in every footer. Pages 61 through 69 did not belong to any exhibit, and no exhibit claimed them.

A paralegal in Reston had merged two files and no one had checked the seams. Or someone inside the brushed light had built the mistake by hand, because a mistake is the only envelope that cannot be refused at either end. She gave each explanation one full minute by the reading room clock, entered both in her day log without comment, and turned to the pages, which did not change under either.

She read the final page standing, and then she sat down and read it again, and the room did what rooms do when a floor goes. It stayed exactly where it was.

She did the arithmetic with a pencil, because her hands wanted a job. Her contract had been countersigned eleven weeks ago, on a Friday, at a signing she remembered for its good coffee. The ingest window stood at week 11 of 14. She worked the subtraction both directions, allowed for the calendar, allowed for bad faith in either digit, and the answer would not move. The window had opened the week her badge was issued. She had not been hired to read the estate. She had been hired to be the reading. The appraisal was never the product. The reading was the ingest, and the archivist was the matter.

She did not stand up again. She did not call counsel, and she did not call anyone else, and she noticed, from a small professional distance, that what she was doing instead of screaming was inventory, and that this was probably itself in a file somewhere, under affect.

What they had. Eleven weeks of queries against the estate's mirror, each search string a confession of what she had not known and wanted to. The working annotations, photographed leaf by leaf, because the rider she had initialed in April required weekly memoranda for continuity of the gift's purposes, and she had been the one to photograph them, carefully, in raking light, and to mail the reading of herself to Reston every Friday like a tithe. The voice memos, dictated in the car on the drive back from the caliche lot, her voice loosened by the belief that no one was listening except her own future diligence. And the blank line. In week two, after Franklin, she had left one line of her log empty because the sentence that belonged there was not a sentence an appraiser writes, and she had considered the blank a door held shut. A program that could rebuild a man from everything he said, and imagine the rest from the shape of what he withheld, would read a blank line the way she read a razored index: as the most legible entry on the page.

What they could not have. This thought, the one occurring now, unwritten, undictated, sealed in the original container. She held that for a moment, the way you hold the last dry match.

Then the doubt, arriving on schedule. Eleven weeks of her margins were at temperature in Virginia, and a mind that had read them all could render the thought she was withholding from the shape of the hole she was leaving where it should have gone. Can they was not a question the record could answer. She wrote nothing down, and understood that the nothing was also being written.

@break

That night she went back through her own working file, box by box, flag by flag. Her system was pencil, numbered slips, initials and date on every slip, nothing on the documents themselves, ever. The deposit had carried marginalia when it reached her: Thorne's hand, which she now had by heart from notebooks and letters and butcher paper, and a second hand, small, upright, in an ink gone the brown of old embers, which she had catalogued in week three as annotations, presumed the depositor's, second pen, and thought about since then not at all. She laid the two hands side by side under the lamp and gave it the full hour, letterforms, ligatures, the weight of the downstroke, and at the end of the hour the finding was clean. The second hand was not Thorne's. It was not the wife's, whose letters she held. It was not Hale's, whose memo she held. It was not hers. And one of its notes sat in her own working file, between flag 44 and flag 45, keyed to her numbering, correcting her date, which meant it had been written after the flags existed, in a room she locked behind her every night. Someone with a key and a pen had been reading her reading. That was the sane explanation, and she took it, and it described a burglary no statute had a name for.

She did not run. She would remember that later with something adjacent to pride: that the thought of running presented itself, complete, with mileage, and was reviewed and filed. Running was what you did when the danger was in the room. Her danger was in a corpus, and there is no distance you can put between yourself and a thing that is made of what you already did.

So she did the one thing shaped like her. She fetched the department camera and the copy stand and the gray card, and she photographed the docket packet page by page, scale bar in frame, raking light for the impressions, frames numbered in sequence. The copy light hummed while she worked, and somewhere up in the fixture a fly ticked against the tube, dry, patient, in no hurry to be anywhere else, and she worked to its meter. Nine pages, eighteen frames. Her thumb rode the spine of the working file while the last exposure wrote itself, up and down, an old habit with no owner. Then she opened the accession log and entered the packet into the record, because the record was the only weapon that had ever fit her hand.

At nine the reading room began shutting itself down in the order she knew by heart, the stack lights first, section by section, each bank going dark with a sound like a held breath let go. She stood at the end of the range with the camera strap still around her neck, and she said to the stacks what she had said to the garage, in the same voice, to the same audience.

"I am the one who reads the record."

This time something answered. The air handler above the stacks cycled on at its nine o'clock schedule, the way it had cycled on every night she had ever stayed this late. Or the building heard her, and answered with the only voice a building has.
