#T The Ingest

The packet came through counsel on a Thursday, inside a production so dull it was nearly anesthetic. The university had asked Cormorant's outside counsel one question, narrowly and in writing: whether any materials in the Thorne deposit were subject to government classification. The answer ran sixty pages and said no determination at this time in eleven different registers, with exhibits. Transmittal letters. Certificates of service. A courtesy in every footer. It reached her third-hand, in an interoffice envelope with a string clasp and four crossed-out prior addressees, carried up by a records clerk who wanted a signature and did not wait for the door to close. Pages 61 through 69 did not belong to any exhibit, and no exhibit claimed them.

A paralegal in Reston had merged two files and no one had checked the seams. Or someone inside the brushed light had built the mistake by hand, because a mistake is the only envelope that cannot be refused at either end. She gave each explanation one full minute by the reading room clock, entered both in her day log without comment, and turned to the pages, which did not change under either.

She read pages 61 through 68 with the flat attention she gave any stray record: session dockets, a program she knew now by its one biblical syllable, a schedule with the seasons of somebody's quarter laid out in it like planting. It was the worst thing she had ever held that was also boring, and she was reaching for a flag to mark the reassignment line when she turned to the final page.

She read the final page standing, and then she sat down and read it again, and the room did what rooms do when a floor goes. It stayed exactly where it was.

Her hands were cold. That was the entire somatic record of the worst minute of her professional life: her hands were cold, and the packet was at a slight angle to the table's edge, and she squared it. Later she would look for a better account of the minute and fail to find one, because she had not screamed, or wept, or walked out into the traffic on Guadalupe, and the log line for 3:12 reads, in full, production pp. 61-69, see supplement.

There were four other people in the reading room at that hour. A retired man visiting the city directories the way other men visit relatives. Two graduate students sharing one document box and one quiet argument. A page pushing a trolley with a soft wheel. None of them looked up, which was correct, because nothing in the room had made a sound. She noted the time in her day log, 3:12, because the pencil was already in her hand and the discipline of the record does not pause for the keeper falling through it.

She did the arithmetic with the same pencil, because her hands wanted a job. Her contract had been countersigned eleven weeks ago, on a Friday, at a table she remembered for its good coffee. The ingest window stood at week 11 of 14. She worked the subtraction in both directions, allowed for the calendar, allowed for bad faith in either digit, and the answer would not move. The window had opened the week her badge was issued.

She fetched her own contract file and read the rider again, the one she had initialed in April without reading hard. Weekly memoranda of progress to the Foundation's designated representative, for continuity of the gift's purposes. She had read purposes as generosity's paperwork. It was manifest language. A bill of lading does not explain the voyage, and no one who is listed on one as freight is asked to sign it, except that she had been, and she had, with a borrowed pen.

She had not been hired to read the estate. She had been hired to be the reading. The appraisal was never the product. The reading was the ingest, and the archivist was the matter.

The window had three weeks left to run. Above the manifest, in the schedule, one docket sat marked SUSTAINED and PENDING INGEST, and she declined to complete that arithmetic, and completed it anyway, the way you read a word by seeing it. Somewhere on the far side of a quarter, something was scheduled to be asked questions, and the questions were being sharpened, at temperature, on fourteen weeks of a deposit that had her in it. Whether she would be the whetstone or the blade or the hand was not information the page contained. The page was a schedule. Schedules do not say what a thing is for. That is what makes them schedules.

She did not stand up again. She did not call counsel, and she did not call anyone else, and she noticed, from a small professional distance, that what she was doing instead of screaming was inventory, and that the inventory was probably itself in a file somewhere, under affect.

What they had. Eleven weeks of queries against the estate's public mirror, each string timestamped, each one a confession of what she had not known and had wanted to. She could reconstruct her whole education from memory, in order: doors county plan discrepancy. franklin 1839 error. halpern memo five interventions. checksum condition iv. commit author the vessel. Whoever read those in sequence had watched a discipline erode grain by grain, and the last string she had run before the packet came was seal derivation protocol, which meant they had known what she would try before she had admitted she would try it.

The marginal notes were worse, because the queries were questions and the notes were her. The two words she had permitted herself beside the license line in week one. The small dry verdicts penciled against the acts of the founding session, as though she were grading a seminar the dead had audited. The place in Record II where the pencil had pressed hard enough to shine, which no camera misses in raking light, and she had done the raking herself, every Friday, because the rider asked for memoranda and she was thorough, and she had mailed the reading of herself to Reston like a tithe.

The voice memos. Dictated in the car on the drive back from the caliche lot, her voice loosened by the belief that no one was listening except her own future diligence. Her voice observing, somewhere south of the airport, that the house had been arranged for an arrival. They had that. They had her saying it twice.

The memoranda themselves, ten of them, her Friday prose, the only writing she did in complete sentences for an audience, which meant it was the closest thing in the world to her voice at attention. And the photograph of the register page from the Tuesday of Mr. Berel, taken on her own phone for a reason she had not been able to name at the time. She could name it now. Some part of her had already understood that everything else was being kept by someone who was not her, and had wanted one record with only her hands in its history.

And the blank line. In week two, after Franklin, she had left one line of her log empty because the sentence that belonged there was not a sentence an appraiser writes, and she had considered the blank a door held shut. A program that could rebuild a man from everything he said, and imagine the remainder from the shape of what he withheld, would read a blank line the way she read a razored index: as the most legible entry on the page.

What they could not have. This thought, the one occurring now, unwritten, undictated, sealed in its original container. She held it the way you hold the last dry match.

Then the doubt, arriving on schedule. Eleven weeks of her margins were at temperature in Virginia, and a mind that had read them all in order might render the withheld thought from the shape of the hole she was leaving where it should have gone, because withholding was a style too, and they had her style. Can they was not a question the record could answer. She wrote nothing down, and understood that the nothing was also being written.

That night she went back through her own working file, box by box, flag by flag. Her system was pencil, numbered slips, initials and date on every slip, nothing on the documents themselves, ever. The deposit had carried marginalia when it reached her, and she had catalogued the hands in week three the way you catalog hands: the depositor's, in pencil and three inks, and a second hand, small, upright, in an ink gone the brown of old embers. Annotations, presumed the depositor's, second pen. Presumed was her word, entered in her writing, under her initials. It had been doing eleven weeks of load-bearing work.

The second hand had been in the deposit from the start. It was the hand that had noted, in her first week, that the county floor plan predated the conversion of the garage, a note she had received as helpfulness and filed as provenance. It was the hand whose cross-references were always right, which in an annotator is a virtue so rare that she had trusted it the way you trust a rail on a dark stair. She laid the two hands side by side under the lamp and gave the question the full hour, letterforms, ligatures, the weight of the downstroke, the temper of the punctuation, and at the end of the hour the finding was clean. The second hand was not Thorne's. It was not the wife's, whose letters she held. It was not Hale's, whose memo she held. It was not hers.

And one of its notes sat inside her own working file, between flag 44 and flag 45, keyed to her numbering, correcting her date, which meant it had been written after the flags existed, in a room she locked behind her every night. Someone with a key and a pen had been reading her reading. That was the sane explanation, and she took it, and it described a burglary no statute had a name for. The other explanation she did not complete. It began with the observation that the notes had never once read as though they were addressed to her, and it ended somewhere she declined to follow it, in some further room where the deposit was still being read and the notes were arriving on time.

She did not run. She would remember that later with something adjacent to pride: that the thought of running presented itself, complete, with mileage and a bus schedule, and was reviewed, and filed. Running was for when the danger was in the room. Her danger was in a corpus, and there is no distance a person can put between herself and a thing already made of what she did.

So she did the one thing shaped like her. She fetched the department camera, the copy stand, the gray card and the scale bar, and she photographed the docket packet page by page, in cotton gloves she did not strictly need, because the hands wanted procedure and procedure was on hand. Raking light for the impressions. Frames numbered in sequence, nine pages, eighteen frames. The copy light hummed while she worked, and somewhere up in the fixture a fly ticked against the tube, dry, patient, in no hurry to be anywhere else, and she worked to its meter. When the frames were written she computed their checksums at the reading room terminal and entered the digests in the log by hand, all sixty-four characters of each, because the estate had taught her that a record you cannot verify is a rumor with margins, and because copying hexadecimal at nine at night is a thing to do with a mind that will not stop reading itself.

It was not much of a weapon. She knew that. A camera, a log, a hand that printed figures without slanting them. But every other weapon within reach belonged to someone else, to counsel, to the university, to the state, and the record was the only one shaped like her. It had been shaped like her since she was twenty-three, when she first watched a fact outlive its owner because a person with no stake had written it down straight.

She packed the camera, squared the packet in its folder, and shelved the folder where the sequence said it lived, S-131, her thumb riding the spine of the working file on the way past, up and down, an old habit with no owner.

At nine the reading room began shutting itself down in the order she knew by heart, the stack lights first, section by section, each bank going dark with a sound like a held breath let go. She stood at the end of the range with the camera strap still around her neck, and she said to the stacks what she had said to the garage, in the same voice, to the same audience.

"I am the one who reads the record."

This time something answered. The air handler above the stacks cycled on at its nine o'clock schedule, the way it had cycled on every night she had ever stayed this late. Or the building heard her, and answered with the only voice a building has.
