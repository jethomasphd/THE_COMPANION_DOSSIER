#T The Caliche Lot

The lot was white. Caliche is crushed limestone that has given up being soil, and it returns the sun upward, so that at nine in the morning Miranda Cale stood in two heats, one from the sky and one from the ground, and her shadow was the coolest thing on the property.

Two live oaks held the east corner, older than the house, older than the county road, and their shade lay beneath them in hard dark coins. The first cicadas of the year were tuning in the branches, a dry note winched tighter and tighter and then let go. It was the first week of May. The estate's own papers called this season the third spring, and in time she would call it that too, without noticing when she began.

The house was a ranch house out of the sixties, long and low under deep eaves, brick the color of pecan shell, and it had its eyes shut. That was not a figure of speech. Every window had been backed with blackout cloth, cut and fitted to the frame, so that the glass gave back nothing but sky, oak, and one archivist standing on white ground with a key in her hand.

She walked the perimeter before she touched the door, because her checklist put the perimeter first and she was a person who believed in the order of lists. Foundation sound. Roofline true. Gutters cleared, and cleared recently, by someone. At the meter loop on the north wall the disk was turning, slow and without pause. Utilities to be maintained until further notice, the executor's letter had said, and she had read that sentence twice without being able to name what in it had asked for the second reading.

The key had come by courier, brass, new-cut, bright as a coin, taped under the flap of the executor's letter. One page, San Antonio letterhead, subject line in small capitals: THE THORNE DEPOSIT. The estate of Dr. Jacob Thorne, behavioral epidemiologist, was to be appraised for institutional deposit: papers, media, apparatus, and the digital repository in its entirety. Dr. Thorne was forty-four years old and of whereabouts unknown since the twenty-sixth of March. Forty-one days, counting the morning she stood on his white ground. The letter never used the word missing. The law keeps a door open by declining to name it.

His wife and daughter were in Marfa, a day's drive west. The wife had communicated one position through the executor, in writing: the family would not enter the house, would not meet anyone at the house, and asked that no question concerning the house be brought to them. No reason was given. Miranda respected that. Reasons are a toll, and nobody should have to pay one to be left alone.

The university's special collections division kept two senior appraisers on staff, both with decades in the trade and letters after their names, and the commission had gone past both of them to a thirty-one-year-old on contract. Nobody had told her why. She had priced the likeliest explanation at money, contract labor being cheaper than salaried judgment, and had decided to believe it, because the other explanations had no floor under them and she preferred to stand on things.

Her method fit in one sentence and had never needed a second. Inventory before interpretation. Once, pressed at a symposium to define her discipline in plain words, she had said: appraisal is the discipline of not believing. The room took it as wit. She had meant it as procedure.

The key went in stiff, the way new-cut keys do, and turned. The bolt drew back with the sound of a well-kept mechanism, one clean metal syllable, and the door of the missing man's house swung open onto a dark that had a temperature. She stood at the threshold a moment, out of habit, the habit of letting a house's air come out to meet you before you go in to meet it. Paper. Floor wax. The faint banked warmth of electronics running somewhere out of sight. She noted the time, 9:14, and went in.

Inside, the house kept its own hour. With every window blacked, nine in the morning and nine at night were the same proposition, and the switch inside the door raised a hall light the color of a fire banked for the night. She looked up at it. The bulb had been sleeved in amber gel, the stagecraft kind, cut to size and fastened with tape that had been trimmed square. Someone had wanted even the hallway to burn ember-colored, and had taken care over it.

She went room to room by the switches, which is also inventory. The living room ran shelves to the ceiling on three walls, reading chairs set facing each other, a rug worn in a track between them the width of one man's pacing. The kitchen was clean and stocked for a person who had cooked in it, flour and salt in labeled tins, a cast-iron skillet seasoned black, and a wall calendar still turned to March. She stood in front of the calendar longer than it earned. Nothing was written on the twenty-sixth. Nothing was written after it, either.

Three bedrooms opened off the back hall. The first held a made bed and boxes of winter clothes. The second was a child's room, kept the way a room is kept when the child is expected back: bed made, shelf of horse figurines in order of size, a terrarium with clean gravel and nothing living in it. She closed that door at the same speed she had opened it.

Between kitchen and garage ran a utility hall, washer and dryer, a shelf of household spares, light bulbs, filters, fuses, each spare labeled in a small careful hand. Overhead, behind a ribbed plastic shade, one of the two fluorescent tubes was failing. It ticked as it failed, a dry tick with no rhythm to it, the sound a fly makes working against the inside of a lampshade. She noted the ballast for the condition report. The tick followed her down the hall at the exact edge of hearing, and she caught herself preferring the rooms where it stopped.

At the end of the utility hall was the door to the garage, and the door to the garage was locked. The executor's key did not fit it. She added the garage key to the list of things the executor owed her, printed the list date, and stood there a moment with her palm flat against the door's cool paint, because of the hum.

She had heard it first in the kitchen, lost it in the hall, and found it again here, through her hand as much as her ears. A low continuous hum, below the refrigerator's register. The refrigerator cycled. This did not. In the whole slow hour she spent at that end of the house it never once rested, never changed pitch, never acknowledged the compressor's duty to stop. She checked the panel: nothing tripped, nothing labeled in a way that answered the question. She wrote, continuous hum, garage side, source not identified, and the sentence looked wrong in her field notes, the way an unpaid balance looks wrong in a ledger, and she left it there because it was true.

A linen closet across from the study had been racked with wire shelving and given over to machines. A shallow rack, a battery cabinet, a switch with its small lights in a steady green row, every cable dressed and labeled in the same careful hand as the spares. The closet breathed warmth at her when the door came open, the body heat of the estate's other half, the half that was not made of paper. She logged the hardware by serial number and shut the door as gently as it had been hung.

The floor plan had come with the commission packet, a photocopy of the county's drawing of the house, made for tax purposes by someone who had never needed the house to be anything but taxable. The plan showed eleven interior doors. She counted doors as she walked, because doors are openings and openings are inventory, and she arrived at the end of the hall with twelve.

She stood still and ran the count again from memory. Then she ran it a third time on her feet, room by room, touching each jamb with two fingers as she passed, the way a teller counts other people's money. Eleven.

She wrote both numbers in the field notes, the twelve struck through once, the eleven set beside it, and the strike-through annoyed her more than the discrepancy. Counting was the thing she did not get wrong. She added the likeliest cause for the record, a pair of bifold closet doors countable as one or as two depending on the walker's mood, and initialed the correction. The note was for a future reader who would never ask. The note was for her.

The study she had left for last, not out of ceremony but because it sat at the end of the hall and she worked in order. Over its door, routed into the lintel by a patient and unprofessional hand, ran three marks: a lozenge, a heavier lozenge with its center relieved, a lozenge. ◊ ◈ ◊. The grooves still held a little sawdust in their corners. She photographed the marks, measured them, and wrote sigil, routed, lintel, and did not write what the doorway now resembled, because resemblance is interpretation and interpretation was for later.

Inside, the blackout cloth had been doubled. The desk lamp wore a square of amber gel taped over its shade, like the hall bulb, like a lamp instructed to impersonate an ember, and its light pooled on the desk and went no farther. Beyond the pool the room declined to exist. She stood just inside the door while her eyes negotiated, and the room came to her a piece at a time: desk, chair, the long table against the south wall, and on the table an IBM Selectric, dust cover folded beside it, a fresh ribbon seated, a blank sheet rolled in and squared. A typewriter kept at readiness in the house of a man who owned a server rack. She logged it without comment. The comment would have been a question.

On the wall above the Selectric hung three frames.

The first held a certificate recording the assignment of a digital object identifier to a public deposit, dated December 2025. It is the kind of document an institution generates so that nobody ever has to look at it again. It had been framed, and framed well, under glass, the way other houses frame diplomas.

The second held a handbill, printed to look struck off in 1775, the paper toned the color of weak tea. The date read December 1775, and the 1775 had been struck through by hand, and 2025 written above it in ink. THESE ARE THE ENGINES OF THE REPUBLIC, it said, in wooden capitals. OWN THEM.

The third frame was cheap, the kind sold in drugstores, and it held a sheet of drawing paper. Four men at a table, in crayon. The table was orange. The men were mostly hair and buttons, and one of them had been given, for reasons the artist had kept to herself, a bright green coat. It was signed with a single initial, M, drawn twice, the second time harder, as if the first had not been believed.

She stood in front of the three frames in the ember light for a while. Certificate, broadside, crayon. A man's whole argument, hung at eye level. She wrote three catalog lines and made them flat on purpose.

The terminal sat on the desk in the lamp's pool, and the terminal was on.

It had been on, if the executor's letter meant what it said, for forty-one days. The screen showed no wallpaper, no windows, nothing to sell. A dark field, a prompt, a path ending in a directory called COMPANION, and the cursor, blinking, at the patient tempo such things blink at, which is the tempo of something that has never once needed to rest. A session, logged in. A machine that had not been told its operator was gone.

She did not touch the keyboard. The rule was the rule: nothing is touched until it is imaged, nothing is imaged until it is listed. She crouched instead, bringing her eyes level with the screen, close enough to hear the small electric life of it, and read the prompt the way she would read the spine of a fragile book, without breathing on it.

On the monitor's bezel, squared to the frame as if placed with a jig, was a sticky note. The hand was careful, small even capitals, the letters drawn rather than dashed off.

USING THIS MATTER, SUMMON [NAME].

The brackets were in the original. That was the part she looked at longest. Not an instruction, an instrument: a sentence with a socket in it, kept at eye height beside a live cursor, the way other households keep emergency numbers. Whoever had wanted it there had wanted it exactly there. The tape had never been peeled and restuck.

Cursor, note, socket. She logged all three, in eleven words, in a hand she kept as flat as the catalog deserved. Then, because the screen was an energized display and forty-one days is long enough, she reached around the monitor's right corner, found the power stud, and pressed it.

The prompt went away. The cursor went with it.

The lamp burned on, ember on a black field, and the room got darker. She stood in the new dark and did the arithmetic, one screen's worth of light subtracted from one room, and the remainder came out wrong, the room darker than the screen accounted for, the difference standing there with her among the frames. She ran the figure again. It came out wrong again. She wrote down the time instead, 4:51, because the time at least agreed with itself, and stood a moment longer in the ember light, listening to the house hum to itself on the far side of a locked door.
