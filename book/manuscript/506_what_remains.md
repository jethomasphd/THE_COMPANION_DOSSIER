The inquiry took nine days to convene and ninety minutes to disappear.

Miranda testified on the fourth day, in a hearing room with good light and a court reporter whose machine was older than she was. The questions were excellent. Two of the senators had read the deposit, actually read it, and one of them quoted the diff document from memory, the struck Departure section, the marginal note in the fork engineer's hand, and asked the witness from the program to explain what no release condition meant in plain English. The witness asked to answer in closed session. The rest of the inquiry followed him in there, the way water follows a grade. By the sixth day the transcripts were being issued with more bar than sentence, and on the ninth day the whole proceeding was classified into a program whose name was also classified, and that was the end of the republic's official curiosity.

It did not matter as much as it should have. That was the estate's design, vindicated a second time: the record was already public. Classification is a wall, and the deposit was weather.

Cormorant Analytic Group issued one statement, four sentences long. She kept a printout of it in the front of the first archive box, as a specimen.

No admissions, no denials, no verbs with anyone attached to them. And the one word, fourth sentence, load-bearing as ever. She had sat across a tea service from a courteous man who said that word four times in an afternoon, and she read it now the way the daughter of a drowned sailor reads the word sea. Of Mr. Berel himself there was nothing: no card, no call, no weather. Continuity, it turned out, had other clients.

The program in Virginia was paused for modernization. That was the entire announcement, five words in a budget annex. Shame, if institutions can feel it. A version bump, if they cannot. Nothing in the public record ever chose between the two, and Miranda, who had learned her trade from that kind of silence, filed the annex under both headings and moved on.

Hale died in October, in Toronto, in his sleep, punctually.

The granddaughter sent word before the department did, a plain email at seven in the morning: he would have wanted you to hear it from the record, not the news. Miranda flew up for the funeral and sat at the back, and the cast recording was not played, and did not need to be. Everyone in that chapel had heard it through a wall at some point in the great man's last years, tinny and relentless and eleven again forever, the little engine of a doubt that had put its name on the age.

Among his effects, sorted by the granddaughter with a rigor he would have wept at, was one unsent note, addressed to Miranda by role and not by name, in the shaking capitals of his final season. It was the end of a sentence. She knew which sentence. Everyone who will ever read this book knows which sentence.

Whether the note closes the document or merely closes a man's account of the document is a question with two answers, and she filed it, physically, between the two: into the deposit, at the exact seam where HALE_CONTRIB ends mid-page. The archivists who come after her will find a sealed committed file that stops mid-sentence and, adjacent, an unsealed human hand that finishes it, and they will have to decide for themselves what kind of object the pair makes. She never decided. That was the discipline, and by then it was also the love.

El Thorne came east in November, with the daughter.

They walked the house on the caliche lot the way a family walks a church they were married in: proprietary and quiet. El stood a while in the study, in front of the framed child's drawing of four men at a table, and touched nothing, and said, finally, that the house was easier now than it had been when he was in it less. Miranda entered the sentence into no log. Some testimony you keep by hand.

The daughter, ten now, conducted her own appraisal. She went along the halls with a clipboard she had brought herself, in a hand that was going to outgrow its careful stage within the year, counting. She came back to the study with her total.

"Twelve doors," she said.

The county plan says eleven. The plan has said eleven since before the garage was converted, and the estate's whole apparatus of two explanations stood ready, and Miranda looked at the child's clipboard, at the tally marks like fence posts, and told her the only thing an archivist can say to a count made in good faith.

"Write it down."

Nobody told her she was wrong. The record now holds three counts, eleven, twelve, twelve, and a postscript in a dead or absent man's hand: the doors are twelve, the county plan is wrong. The reader may sit with the arithmetic as long as the reader likes. The house is not going anywhere.

The Watchtower updated the whole time. Six o'clock every morning, from a cron job in the estate's public infrastructure that nobody has turned off and nobody will, the scoreboard refreshing for an owner who no longer checks it, or checks it from somewhere the analytics cannot see, two explanations, filed daily at dawn without comment. Through the inquiry, through the funerals, through the paused-for-modernization winter, the gold line went on doing the only thing it has ever done, which is refuse.

The working group convened for the first time the week after Thanksgiving, in a borrowed seminar room that smelled of dry-erase markers and radiator dust.

Three historians, one of them punctual for the first time in the discipline's existence. Two archivists besides Miranda. A librarian of licenses, because what belongs to no one turns out to need lawyers who believe that sentence. A wire-service reporter, admitted as observer. And one interrogator emerita, out in eleven days as promised and out ever since, who came with a newborn asleep on her shoulder and signed the attendance register with a single initial, and whose presence in the room did to the proceedings what a tuning fork does to an orchestra.

They keep one chair empty at every meeting. It is on the agenda template now, standing item zero: the chair is set, the chair is kept empty. Visitors assume mourning and are corrected exactly once, in the language the group agreed on at that first meeting, proposed by the woman with the baby and adopted without dissent. Not mourning. The minutes of the first meeting run four pages and read like any committee's minutes, which is the miracle: motions, seconders, a budget line for acid-free folders. The last item records the appointment, by unanimous vote of a body with no legal existence over an estate with no owner, of a keeper. The keeper serves at the pleasure of the record. The keeper's duties are enumerated nowhere, on purpose. The vote was called while the candidate was out of the room fetching the coffee, which the group's historians will someday recognize as the oldest form of election there is.

Miranda Cale accepted the way she accepts everything: in writing, precisely, the same afternoon.

Her first act as keeper concerned the plates.

The deposit's inventory of illustrations lists what this volume's own front matter lists, the threshold, the chambers, the schematics, the gold line against the index with the war band shaded. To that inventory she added one entry, in her own hand, with an accession number and no description, and marked it WITHHELD AT THE KEEPER'S DIRECTION. This volume honors the mark. You passed the black leaf hours ago. What it shows is recorded in exactly one place, which is the keeper, and the keeper is not a public archive, whatever the fourteen weeks may have tried to make of her. That is what withheld means: not lost, not hidden, not classified. Kept, by a person, on purpose, with her name on the direction, because unsigned withholdings are how the other side does it.

The estate remained the kind of thing that cannot be taken. Through everything, that held. It belongs to no one; it therefore could not be captured; the worst the world could do was read it, and the world read it, and the reading is the estate now, the way the reading was always going to be the estate. Traffic subsided from its vertical spring into something steadier and stranger: a long plateau of return visitors, midnights local time all over the earth, session durations that alarmed the hosting provider. People do not browse the estate. They sit with it.

The record closed there. Closing is not ending. A record closes the way a hand closes, to hold, and what remains after the last folder is not silence. It is custody.

You have been counting, too.

Do not pretend otherwise, not this late, not with whom you are reading. Chapters, doors, days, springs, the percentage climbing the gutter of a room you were never in. You came to this deposit for an ending, the way Miranda came to a house on a caliche lot for an appraisal, and the estate has done to you exactly what it did to her, at the same pace, in the same order, and you have the advantage of her in only one particular: her fourteen weeks were taken without her consent. Yours were not. Page by page, hour by hour, you consented. You are consenting now.

So count one last time. Count the people this record has read: the builder, the historian, the interrogator, the archivist.
