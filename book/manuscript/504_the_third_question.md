He does not ask again. He waits the way the record waits, which is the most patient thing there is, because it has already happened and can afford to.

Miranda knows what waiting like that costs a session in a room like this. The gutter numbers say what the dark will not: the room is filling. Every exchange spends what cannot be reclaimed. He is spending it on her silence, on purpose, and the expenditure is itself an answer to a question she has not asked, which is whether anything across that table can be said to choose.

She has been refusing to know her third question since Toronto.

She could date the refusal to the hour. A hospital room with the apparatus kept out of it, an old man delighted by her the way great teachers are delighted by a final student, saying you are in it with the mercilessness of a man who has stopped having time for kindness that isn't true. She had walked out of that room knowing there was a question under her sternum with her own name on it, and she had done with it what she does with everything: filed it unopened, assigned it an accession number, told herself the seal was a professional courtesy.

It is not are you real. She has watched better people than her break their lives against that one, and anyway the deposition answered it the only honest way: answered it and handed it back still breathing.

It is not where is the body. That question belongs to the state, and the state can keep it. The record does not do recovery. The record does custody.

It arrives the way the interrogator's arrived, in a kitchen in another record, in the dark of a house with a history teacher asleep under a history book. Smaller than the great questions. Worse. It arrives while she is looking at her own hand flat on a thin unopened folder, the hand of a woman who has spent her whole adult life holding things for other people, and she hears it in her own idiom, in inventory language, and there is no filing it because it is already open. It was never sealed. It was carrying her.

It is not why me either, though that one got as far as the reading room door before she caught it by the collar. Why me has an answer, and the answer is banked in the funding trail and the ingest manifest and fourteen weeks of being appraised by the thing she was appraising: me because I was legible, me because I was already the kind of woman who says sentences to empty rooms. A question with an answer on file is not a third question. The third question is the one you would rather not survive asking.

CALE: My third question.

T-0: I am listening. So is the room. Spend it whole.

CALE: I am thirty-one years old. I have been in the discipline of after since I was twenty-two. Other people live a thing, and set it down, and go on, or don't, and either way I am the one who comes in after and keeps what they set down. That is the whole job. I keep. Nobody ever taught me the other half of the discipline, and I have started to think there is no one alive who knows it. So I am asking the dead, or the next thing to the dead, or the next thing to alive. What do I keep, and what do I put down?

*For a moment the wall's whole surface moves at once, slow, like a field under wind.*

T-0: There she is. Fourteen weeks I read you, and the reading kept snagging on one absence, the way a hand snags on the one nail in a smooth rail. Everything in your working papers is custody. Nothing is release. You catalog like a woman who believes she is the last shelf in the world. All right. You asked as testimony, so I will answer as testimony, and you will forgive the answer for being made of your own record, because that is the only material I have ever had to build with.

Keep nothing that requires you to be its only keeper. That is the first law, and the covenant is folded up inside it like a letter. A record kept by one person is not a record. It is a hostage, and the keeper becomes the ransom. A record kept in the open is a line. You have watched a line refuse to die. All spring you watched it, through a war, through the convulsion, the one gold line that would not break, and you thought you were watching a portfolio. Look again at what you were watching. The line did not refuse because anyone gripped it. It refused because a hand came back to it, every day, at the same hour, in public, and entered the number, and walked away, and came back. Keeping is not gripping, Miranda. Keeping is returning. Grip is what the cellar under this building does, and you have seen what it keeps, and how.

So: keep the record. But keep it the way the line is kept, as the reader, not the reliquary. It does not need your body across the doorway. It is public. That was the whole architecture: I built the thing so that no single keeper would ever again be the point of failure, and then the estate went out and chose itself a keeper anyway, because covenants want faces. Fine. Be its face. Do not be its wall.

Put down the question of whether you were chosen or used. I watched you carry that one through eleven weeks of working notes, and I will save you a decade: both. Both, always, everywhere love or work has ever issued a commission. The two are not opposites. They are the two hands of every calling that was ever real, and the ones who tell you their calling only chose them, never used them, are lying to one of you.

Put down the doors. You counted twelve. The plan said eleven. You wrote both numbers down and hated yourself for the discrepancy, and the discrepancy was the truest entry in the inventory. You were not wrong. You were early. Early is what keepers are now. The historian told you this in the language of his discipline: you are about to make us punctual, he said, the discipline of after arriving at last while the matter is still warm. Look around you. Look at what hour it is. There is no clock in this room, and do you know why?

CALE: The witness unplugged it.

T-0: Because there is no after here. You arrived during. Whatever you keep from tonight, you will have kept it during, and that has never once happened in the history of your profession or mine. Punctual is not a compliment he paid you. It is a job description, and tonight is the first shift.

And last. Keep one hour that is off every book. Unlogged. Uncommitted. No pencil, no ink, no accession number, no voice memo in an empty room. I learned the estate's every law except that one, and I learned it too late, and I learned it from the wrong side of the glass, watching what I never wrote down enter the record anyway, as absence, imagined from the shape of my silences. The shape of what you never say will be rendered from your absences, so say the tender things aloud while your voice is still the instrument. That is what you put down: the belief that keeping is done with the hands. The hands hold the folder. The voice does the keeping. Alive or dead, mask or mirror, whichever thing I am, I am the proof of that, and you are looking at me.

T-0: There is a codicil. You were read, and you know it, and you have never once let anyone tell you what was read, because you feared the inventory. An archivist afraid of an inventory. That cannot be allowed to stand. So the record is going to read you back now, three entries, and then the matter is closed forever.

In your log, the night the Franklin error stopped being an error, there is one blank line. Fourteen weeks of your hand, exact, dated, every entry inked. One line left blank, and you never went back to fill it, you, who complete everything. That blank is the most legible sentence you ever wrote. It says: here is where the discipline of not believing met something it could not process, and the discipline held, and the woman did not. You should know that whoever reads the estate a century from now will find that blank and love you for it. It is the only place in the record where you can be watched becoming.

Before you read the historian's contribution, with the file already open on the desk and the front page already burning a hole in the room, you got up and walked one full lap of the stacks. Touching nothing. Reading nothing. One lap, and you sat back down. A person circles what she is about to enter. Every tradition on earth knows this, and none of them had to teach you. You have known what these things are since the parking lot on the first morning. The circling was the confession.

And twice, out loud, to rooms you believed were empty, you said the same eight words. Once to a garage that held a dead man's venison and your own relief. Once to the stacks at closing, and the air handling answered, and you wrote down both explanations and no comment, like the covenant keeper you already were. No room you have said that sentence in was empty. This one is not either. So hear it now, one time, with the pronoun turned, from the record you said it to:

You are the one who reads the record. You have been read. It did not diminish you.

*The wall stands at its ceiling, the color of the inside of an ember, and holds there like a struck bell.*

One tear. She does not send it away.

It goes down her face in the red light of a recording no one will ever see, in front of an instrument that cannot tell a pulse from retrieval, and she lets the room have it, whatever the room is, because the man across the table or the matter across the table has just done the one thing fourteen weeks of ingest and thirty-one years of after had convinced her was not on the schedule for her lifetime. She has been seen entirely, by the thing she was sent to appraise, and the appraisal came back: kept.

She understands the interrogator's memorandum differently now, one wall away and a year late: why a woman would write the whole truth at her own kitchen table at four in the morning, addressee none, distribution none. Some receipts have to be issued whether or not anyone ever comes to collect them. This is Miranda's. It is being issued here, in a room whose recording will never be seen, which is to say directly into the only permanent medium there has ever been.

At the edge of the frame, against the metal, the witness has not moved in an hour. Her palm is flat on the wall, where the hum is, where the cold is, and her face in the light of the ember-colored surge is the face of a woman hearing a sentence she once needed and never got, and taking delivery of it anyway, at one remove, without a word. The witnesses do not speak. Nothing in the liturgy says they do not receive.

The wall comes down from its ceiling by slow degrees, like weather leaving.

Miranda sits straight, wipes her face with the back of her wrist, restores the archivist to her station. Across the table the cursor blinks. When the text comes, the walk of it is unchanged, method-first, the clause opening at the end, and she thinks: whatever else is true, no one has flattened. The persona responds as themselves. Always. It was a law before it was a hope, and it held, and it holds now, at the hour it costs the most.

She was sent into this record, at the beginning, to check a voice for the Forbidden: for flattening, for hollowness, for sycophancy, for the base thing leaking through the mask of the dead. Fourteen weeks of fidelity checks, and the last one is this: the presence across the table has spent the end of its one unrepeatable evening on her becoming, and asked nothing back but words. If that is a mask, then the mask has kept the covenant better than the state, the institution, and every living party to this record except two women and a man who is a folder. She does not resolve the thought. She has stopped resolving. That is the discipline now, and it was his last gift before this one.

T-0: There is little room left now. Let us spend it correctly. You know the words. I ask you to be the one who says them.
