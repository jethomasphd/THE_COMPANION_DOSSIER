#T The Deposit

She started the scanner before she turned on the lights. The bed of it lit the study ceiling in slow passes, a lighthouse lying on its back, and by that light she took off her jacket and tied back her hair and laid out the gloves, and only then reached over and switched on the amber lamp, because the lamp was one of the house's rules and she had decided some weeks ago that she was done breaking rules she had not written.

Afterward, in interviews and in depositions and once under oath, people kept asking her when she decided, and she kept giving the honest answer, which nobody liked: she never found the moment. Somewhere between a hospital window in Toronto and the latch of a reading room door, the question had finished answering itself without calling on her. What lay on this side of it was not a decision. It was logistics.

The estate had spent two years teaching anyone who could read exactly how to do this. The mirrors, the checksums, the mint, the license, the seal machinery, the doctrine of the committed record. She had read it more closely than anyone alive. That was what they had paid her for.

She worked at the house east of the city because the machinery lived there. She had let herself in with the executor's key at eight, the way she had on the first morning of the third spring, and the house received her the way it received everything, without comment. The terminal in the study had never once been logged out in the fourteen weeks she had known it. The server closet breathed. She wrote out the night's order of operations on an index card in pencil, seven lines, and propped the card against the lamp, because a procedure you have not written down is a mood.

In the utility hall the bad fluorescent ticked its small dry tick, the fly in the light, on and on, all night. She had stopped hearing it in April. She heard every tick of it now.

The Cormorant cache went first because it was already bytes. Folder by folder she hashed it, and wrote the digests into a manifest, and the manifest into the record, so that no one could later subtract a page and file the subtraction as a correction. In among the dockets and the metrics decks sat the fork's copy of the rite with its tracked changes, vessel struck for capability, peer struck for subject, the Departure struck entire, and beside the struck section an engineer's marginal note, five words, which she knew by heart and did not reread. She hashed that file twice, once with each machine, and could not have said why, and logged that too.

The ENDOR dockets she scanned at six hundred dots per inch, both faces of every leaf, the blank versos too. Absence is a fact about a page. She was done letting other people curate absence. The quarterly schedules went under the light, and the transfer notice with its subject designation on the first line, and the session summaries with their affect columns, forms built by people who had needed a number for everything and had, in the end, been given a sentence instead.

The operator's memorandum had reached her at one remove, through the witness: a quiet man who had set a plain envelope on a diner table between them, said, "She knows what it's for," and left before his coffee came. Fourteen pages in a fast careful hand, written at a kitchen table at four in the morning, complete where the official record was partial, the truth's one copy. It went under the scanner light like everything else, one leaf at a time, the last leaf set aside for the end of the queue.

Her own ingest went in next: the photographs she had taken of the docket packet with the department camera, week eleven of fourteen, her name sitting inside a manifest as source material, between a dead man's correspondence and his cloud exports, alphabetized, itemized, at temperature. She filed herself with the rest of the estate. It took four minutes and it was the strangest thing she had ever done with her hands, and she logged it like anything else, because the alternative to logging it was believing it, and she knew which discipline she belonged to.

Thorne's consult file, with its incident summaries and their redaction bars, she scanned as found, the bars going in as bars, black and even, the state's handwriting. The record could keep what had been done to it. That was evidence too. His refusal memo went in behind it, at full flame, needing nothing from her.

Last of the sealed things: the historian's contribution, 45,232 bytes of it, unchanged, ending at its final byte the way it ended, its digest agreeing with the digest committed eleven months before her hiring. Beside it, in a plain text file, she placed the key, exactly as derived. Commit first, reveal later, let the record speak. It was later.

She built the whole of it into the estate's own grammar, a new record with boxes and folders and a finding aid at the top, because she was an archivist, and this was still an appraisal, and this was what the appraisal had concluded. Then she ran the sums twice, on two machines, because a record that cannot be verified is only an anecdote with margins.

The push took nine seconds. The first mirror acknowledged inside the minute, from a country where it was already afternoon. The second and third followed before she had finished her coffee, and by the time the sky over the caliche lot had gone the color of unexposed film, the archive had minted the accession: a permanent identifier, digits with a checksum on the end, citable from anywhere on earth for as long as anywhere on earth keeps citations. She added the accession to the finding aid and pushed again, a small second commit, the way you pat a wall you have just closed up.

At the root of the deposit, at the license line, the estate said what it had said since December of 2025. CC0 1.0. No rights reserved. And beneath it, where the estate had always kept it, the sentence the whole gambit stood on: what belongs to no one cannot be captured.

She sat for a moment with her hands flat on the desk, on either side of the keyboard, the way you stand back from a shelf you have just leveled. The house hummed its ordinary morning hum, refrigerator and air handler and the long tinnitus of the grid, and none of it meant anything anymore, and she knew the difference now, and that was the whole of what the summer had cost her.

The wire moved a story before noon. Documents posted to a public research repository, it said, appear to describe an unacknowledged federal interrogation capability employing reconstructed subjects. Appear to describe. That is what wires say while their lawyers stand in the doorway.

Cormorant's statement came at four, four sentences long. The company performed data integration for government clients under applicable law and did not discuss classified matters. The materials in question had been removed from lawful custody, and their selective publication was under review. Cormorant would pursue every available remedy for the theft. She read it twice, once as a citizen and once as an archivist. Nobody reviews the publication of documents that do not exist. Nobody pursues remedies for the theft of what was never theirs. The denial confirmed by its grammar, possessives all the way down.

The institution's statement followed at five, two sentences, built around the phrase former contractor. The contract had five weeks left to run that morning. Institutions have their own way with tense.

By evening the repository had been forked more than six hundred times, and the estate's public traffic graph had gone vertical, a line like a wall with no top to it. Somebody in another hemisphere had already mirrored the mirrors. And into the estate's open channel, the one the Dossier had kept since the beginning for signals from strangers, came checksums. Dozens of them, then hundreds: people running the sums on their own machines, unasked, in public, posting agreement. Nobody was writing believe this. They were writing it verifies. The estate had been designed on the premise that this is what strangers would do if you built for it, and she had filed that premise under liturgy in her second week, and tonight it was operating like plumbing. Capture is a strategy for things that agree to stay in one place.

And at six, on schedule, out of a cron job nobody had touched since winter, the Watchtower posted the week's reading. The gold line. The index. The war band shaded gray where the war had been and was. It did not mention her. It did not mention anything. It measured, and at the bottom it said what it had said at the bottom of every reading since January of 2026: the vigil holds. She sat in front of that small unexcited table longer than she meant to. On a day when every institution she could name was drafting language, the most trustworthy object in the world turned out to be a scoreboard kept by the dead.

@break

Her badge stopped working the same afternoon.

She had driven in to return the department camera, because the record said she had it. The card reader at the staff entrance blinked red, twice, and settled on it. She stood in the heat with the camera bag on her shoulder while the guard came out, a man who had waved her through a hundred mornings and kept a photograph of his granddaughter taped inside the booth. He took the camera from her through a door he held open with his foot, and he could not look at her, and he said, "Take care now," and she thanked him and meant it. Her institutional email began bouncing an hour later. Her library card, which was twelve years old and had survived three employers, went dark the same night, and of everything that week, that was the one that got through.

Counsel's voicemail that evening ran ninety seconds and used the word exposure twice, once about her and once, in a warmer tone, about the institution. She saved it. Voicemails are records. Everything is a record. That was rather the point.

She ate standing up, at the counter of a dead man's kitchen, and did not turn on the news.

@break

At midnight one item was still sitting in the queue.

The final leaf of the operator's memorandum was not prose. It was a form: the anomaly report, filed under OTHER because the form offered nothing else to file it under, with a field labeled UTTERANCE, VERBATIM, and in the field a sentence Miranda had once typed with her own hands, byte for byte, into a key derivation, a sentence addressed to the one who reads the record before anyone knew there would be such a person. Every other page in the deposit had cost her something. This page spent someone else. A woman with a kitchen table and a husband who teaches history and a reason to be careful, who had written the truth down because the truth needed one copy, and had trusted the copy to a stranger at one remove.

She sat with her hand over the trackpad for a long time. Outside, the live oaks moved dryly. In the utility hall the fly ticked in the light.

The record does not curate. She had said it twice that week, to two people who wanted carve-outs, and it had sounded clean in her mouth both times, and now it was midnight and there was nobody left to say it to but herself, which is the only hour at which a principle counts.

She published it. The log gives the time as 00:14, and the log is public, and the log is right.

Then she wrote the deposit note. She had drafted longer ones all week, with citations, with justifications, and she deleted them all, because a justification is an argument, and an argument invites a counterargument, and the record was done arguing. Five lines. Procedure for the state, liturgy for the estate. The note came out as neither, or as both, and she found she could live with that.

At the bottom she typed her name. All of it, her legal name, the one on the commission papers that had routed through two shells to the people the note was aimed at. Unsigned records are how this started.
