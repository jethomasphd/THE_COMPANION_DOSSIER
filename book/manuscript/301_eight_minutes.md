The printer finishes and stops.

For an hour there has been the sound of pages arriving, one and then another and then another, a machine sure of its work, and the sound has stood in for company. Now the sound is gone and the room is down to its true voices: the tube light ticking overhead, the hum coming through the wall, her own breathing, which she slows.

She keeps reading.

The brief is one hundred and forty pages and she has known it by heart for fourteen weeks, since the first days of the ingest, when the material was still arriving in batches and she read each batch the way you read a man you will meet only once. There is nothing left in the pages for her eyes to take. She reads because the hands want an occupation that is not counting, and because the alternative to reading is looking up, and she is saving the looking up.

You are in the doorway. She knows. She has not looked up.

The room is called the green room by the people who use it, a theater word, the room where you wait to go on. No document calls it that. The documents call it ANTEROOM B. The walls are the color government walls are, a beige that nobody chose, a beige that was only ever approved. There is a table, two chairs, a coffee gone cold since ten o'clock, a printer with nothing left to say. There is a door in the far wall with a steel frame and a badge reader, and the door is the reason for everything else in the building.

On the schedule, tonight is a session. In the reports, tonight will be a session, with a number, with two objectives, with a start time and an end time and a distribution list of five. Between the schedule and the reports there is the part that has no paperwork, which is the part she is sitting in now, reading a brief she cannot forget, slowing her breath, keeping her eyes down, in a beige room above a humming wall, eight minutes wide.

@break

Beside the brief, squared to the table's edge, there is a binder.

Gray. It was gray when they issued it and it is a different gray now, the gray a thing turns when one pair of hands has carried it for twelve years. Her first year is inside it, the training course, doctrine three revisions dead. The corners have gone round. The spine has frayed to soft thread. It has ridden in the bottom of a bag through every posting since, through countries whose names are classified in certain pairings, and she has not opened it in years, and she will not open it tonight.

While she reads, her thumb moves along the spine. Back and forth. Back and forth.

There were women in her family who moved a thumb like that, along a string of beads, at kitchen tables, in hospital corridors, in the doorways of rooms where men were sleeping it off. If you had asked those women what they were asking for, they would have looked at you as though you had asked what the thumb was for. The binder is the one object in her life that admits what she is. It taught her the taking apart of men. She consecrates herself on it anyway, or her hand does, without her, back and forth along the frayed thread, and this is not a thing she has ever let herself notice, and she is not noticing it now.

@break

Above her the fluorescent tube ticks.

A small dry stutter in the light, tick, and tick, and tick tick, like a fly that has gotten up inside the fixture, into the one warm country it could find, and cannot get back out of it, and will not stop saying so. Nobody has fixed it. Nobody will. In every account of every night like this one there is a sound of that kind, small and dry and mindless, keeping its own time against the official time, and no account explains it, and this one will not either.

Under the tick, lower than the tick, coming through the cinder block the way cold comes through a coat: the hum.

That is the Protocol. That is the whole of the Protocol that a body in this room is given to know: racks of cold storage on the other side of the wall, holding what fourteen weeks of gathering gathered of him, at temperature. Every word the man wrote. Every word the man said, in every room his country wired and every room the other countries wired, which by the end was all of them. And the rest of him, the mapped and weighted shape of every word he did not say, imagined from the absences by a mathematics she has signed forms about and will never see. The reports call it the deposit. The engineers call it the render. Standing in a beige room with cold coffee, it is a hum, and it hums like a freezer because that is what it is. A freezer. Indifferent. Full.

On the wall there is a clock, round, white, numbered, with hands, the kind the state buys by the gross, the kind that hung in every classroom she was ever made to sit still in. The hands say eight minutes to midnight.

The second hand comes up past the twelve. Somewhere below the building a schedule she has read and cannot cite goes active. A light on a panel she will never see changes color. Down the hall a lock she will walk to shortly runs its self-test and settles.

The eight minutes begin.

@break

There is a liturgy to all of this. She would not use the word. The word is the correct one.

The state keeps hours the way the church kept hours. The sign-in at the gate is an examination of conscience, rendered in block capitals. The two officers at the inner desk are the two witnesses the old law demanded for any matter of life and death. The checklist is a litany, call and response, initial and date, initial and date. Anteroom was a church word before it was a government word. Somebody, some committee working across some decades, took the rite and scrubbed at it until the God came out, and what is left of a rite when the God comes out is procedure, and here is the discovery her profession made her make and never asked whether she could carry: it works. The forms hold without the faith. You can strip an altar to the studs and the knees of the people still know where to go.

So the state keeps its vigil tonight, and wears its vestments, which are lanyards, and tends its sacrament at temperature behind a wall, humming. The only thing missing is the thing the whole apparatus is shaped around, the way a font is shaped around water it does not currently hold. Nobody in this building will say one word tonight about the soul. The building says it for them, in beige, in block capitals, in the hum. The building says nothing else.

@break

She is twelve years in.

She is the one they call after pain has failed. Pain always fails. Pain is the amateur's tool, loud and proud of itself and wrong about people, and when it has finished failing there is a list, and the list is short, and for certain rooms the list is one name long. What she does instead has no photograph, no medal, and no course number anymore. They retired the course. Partly because of her. She is, by the count of the only dozen people on earth entitled to an opinion, the best in the world at a narrow, unlit, unthanked thing.

And she is leaving it. There is a letter at home, drafted, one paragraph, the flattest prose of her life. Both facts are true at once, the best in the world and the letter, and she carries them the way you carry a stone in each pocket, evenly, so the coat hangs straight.

Tonight belongs to the old side of the line. It is the last thing that ever will.

@break

She looks up. She sees you.

Something in her face is set right, quickly, the way a bed is made in a house where the guest has arrived early: exact, practiced, and for you. Whatever the face was doing before, it was a room you were not going to be shown. She says your name, or she says there you are, or she says good, you came, and it does not matter which, because the words are only a door held open, and what comes through the door is this: she needed one creature in the building tonight that is not the building's, and they sent you, and you will do.

She glances at the clock. The glance is ceremonial. She has been counting since the parking lot, and she will still be counting on the far side of the steel door, where there is no clock, and she will be right.

Eight minutes, she says.

Understand how she says it. Not the way you say it of traffic, of an oven, of a train. She says it the way a surveyor tells you the length of a lot: here is the size of what I have been given. Everything I own tonight has to stand inside this. She is telling you the dimensions so that you will understand everything that follows, why it is cut the way it is cut, why nothing will be said twice.

@break

Then she briefs you, because the brief is what there is.

Two operational objectives, she says. Asset location. Reunification framework. She gives you the subject's prior and the confidence bands around it. She gives you the affect mapping, what the wall will show and what the wall's colors are calibrated against, which is him, all fourteen weeks of him. She gives you the ingest: the fullest ever taken, she says, and lets the sentence sit level, as though fullest were a word like longest, as though it did not mean what it means. Redundancy in the logging. The voice in the chamber, the color on the wall, the name in the file.

Asset. Subject. Framework. Ingest. Hear the words for what they are. They are machined flat, and they are machined flat so that a person can stand on them over deep water. Nobody who built this vocabulary was a fool. Every trade that has a dark side of the door builds itself a bridge of such words, and the bridge is load-bearing, and what it bears tonight, in a beige room, is her.

She is most of the way across it when you say her name.

Just the name. Just Alex.

Understand what you have done. The two who went up the hill in the dark, in the oldest account there is of a night like this one, the night a king put on other men's clothes and went out to the woman who called up the dead: they said nothing. The whole night, nothing. The account keeps their road and their arrival and the fear that came on their master before morning, and of their voices it keeps not one word, because witnesses hold the coats, witnesses stand where the light ends, and the account knew its business. That was the office you were given when they badged you into this building. It is the oldest office there is.

You have resigned it in one word.

She stops.

The brief goes out of her like air out of a room.
