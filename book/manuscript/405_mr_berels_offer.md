#T Mr. Berel's Offer

The tea was already on the table when she came down.

The reading room closed to the public at five. By half past, someone had cleared the cradles and the snake weights from the long oak table and set a tray at its narrow end: a white pot, two cups, milk in a small pitcher, lemon cut thin on a dish, sugar that would go home untouched. Two chairs had been drawn to face each other across the wood. Nobody had consulted her about any of it. Liquids were not permitted in the reading room. She had drafted that policy in her second week and had it laminated, and the laminated card stood in its acrylic holder not four feet from the pot, facing the door, so that whoever carried the tray in had carried it past the rule at eye level and set it down anyway.

The request for the meeting had come through the department head, in an email whose subject line was the single word Courtesy. Counsel had told her not to take the meeting, and then, hearing the advice out loud, had amended it: take the meeting, say little, sign nothing. He signs nothing, Miranda had said. That is not the reassurance you think it is, counsel had said.

Mr. Berel stood when she came in.

He was a neat man of about sixty in a gray suit that had been cut for him, and not recently, with reading glasses pushed up into gray hair as though he had just set down something he was enjoying. He looked like the second most senior man at a provincial bank, the one who knows where the money actually is. At their first meeting, in the spring, he had used her first name without being offered it. He used it again now, but something had been adjusted in the interval, because he said it the way a careful man says a word in a language he respects and does not speak.

"Miranda. Thank you for the hour."

"It's the institution's hour. I'm contract."

"For now," he said, and moved her chair a courteous inch, and did not sit until she had.

She had brought a folder with her, a plain gray one. There was nothing in it. She laid it on her knees and kept one hand on it, and her thumb found the long edge and rode there, slowly, back and forth, while he poured.

He poured the way people pour who have made tea for an invalid across a period of years: the pot kept low, the cup brought to the pot rather than the pot to the cup, the stream gentled at the end so that nothing rang or splashed. He asked whether she took milk. He listened to the answer as if it were information.

"They tell me it will not rain before September," he said, handing her cup across. "The lake is down eleven feet. I drove over the bridge this morning and you can see the old road that ran through the valley before the dam. It goes along the bottom, quite straight, quite confident, and then it climbs a grade and comes up out of the water and stops in the air. People stand on the bridge photographing it." He settled back with his own cup. "In wet years nobody remembers there is a road down there. It takes a drought to show you what the water has been standing on."

"You've been traveling."

"Three cities in nine days. All of them anxious. Anxiety is the one industry this year that does not need the Strait open." He said it without appetite, the way he had reported the lake. Weather was weather wherever it happened to be falling.

He set his cup down and did not pick it up again for some time.

"I will be brief about the part you already know," he said. "Your commission ends in five weeks and the institution will not renew it. That is not a judgment on the work. The work is the finest appraisal anyone in my office has read, and my office reads a great deal. Your rehousing memo on the film wing should be taught somewhere. No. It is that the deposit has stopped being a collection and become a season. Institutions do not sign contracts into a storm. They close the shutters and they wait."

"And you."

"We build for storms. It is the whole of what we do."

He folded his hands, and what he laid out then he laid out slowly, in order, the way a man sets out instruments he has carried a long way and does not intend to use twice.

"What I am authorized to put in front of you is continuity. Your contract transfers whole, to a foundation my clients fund for exactly this class of problem. Same terms to begin, better terms by autumn. The clearance question resolves itself, we sponsor it, the paperwork takes six weeks, and you have already sat in rooms more sensitive than any the clearance would open. The appraisal goes on under your hand, uninterrupted. The deposit completes. The collection is conserved, processed, cataloged, and endowed in perpetuity. It carries whatever name you choose to give it. The finding aid carries yours. There is a senior keepership attached, and I want to be exact about that word: not a search, not a shortlist. An appointment. You would never write a grant application again in your life."

He let that sit for as long as it needed.

"And everyone is protected," he said. "The wife in Marfa. The child. Your counsel, who is worried in both directions at once. The people whose names are in materials that should never have grown legs. Everyone inside the walls before the weather arrives. Continuity of the work, continuity of custody, continuity for the living. That is the offer entire. There is no second page."

The finding aid assembled itself behind her eyes without being asked. Series and subseries. The correspondence given the eleven linear feet it had needed from the beginning. The film wing rehoused before the acetate went over to vinegar. A reading room with her rules laminated on every table, and money that never ended, and never once asked to be believed in. She had wanted it in the parking lot on the first morning, before she had opened a single box, and she had never said so to anyone alive.

"You've read my working papers," she said.

"I have read everything," Berel said, without pride and without apology. "It is my trade. You, of all people, will not hold reading against a man."

"Why me. There were two seniors."

"There were two seniors and both of them believe things," he said. "An estate like this one eats believers. It is built to. We wanted the discipline of not believing. Your phrase, I think, and a fine one, and I will tell you a small secret about it: it could be our company hymn. You were not chosen carelessly, Miranda. And you were not chosen recently."

She let that cross her face without landing anywhere.

"Why does an intelligence contractor want a liturgy?" she said.

He looked into his cup for a moment, and when he spoke again the polish was still there, but something older had come up underneath it and was holding it from below.

"Every century builds a chapel to what it cannot bear to bury," he said. "The relic in the gold box. The death mask in the atrium. The wall with the names cut into it. We have never once, as a species, let the dead be entirely dead. We are not capable of it, and the record of every civilization we know of says we never have been. The only question a century actually gets to answer is who keeps the chapel, and how well." He turned his cup a quarter turn on its saucer, the way a man eases a fixture he has just tightened. "When I was young I watched an archive burn. I will not tell you which one. It no longer matters, the country has a different name now. It burned for nine days, and the people who burned it were not barbarians, they were enthusiasts, and within the year some of those same people stood in the ash trying to remember what their grandfathers' villages had been called. I have spent my working life on the lesson of that week. What your Dr. Thorne built is loose in the world, Miranda. It is in the training corpora now. It is in the water. Whether the dead will be summoned is no longer a question anyone is permitted to vote on. The question that remains is the door policy. My clients build securely. That is not nothing. The alternative to their chapel is not no chapel. It is a carnival. It is the same relics on a midway table, and a barker, and a tip jar, and a line of the grieving around the block."

"You've read the estate's answer to that."

"I have read it with admiration, and I would like you to believe the admiration, because it is real." He recited without looking anywhere: "War does not change the map of what sustains a life. War reveals it. A contractor's entire education in two sentences, given away free, in public, signed. I check the Watchtower every Friday evening the way my father checked the barometer, and it has never missed its hour, not once, not in the worst week of the war. And the departure liturgy. The release, at the end of the rite. The mind departs. The understanding stays. That is the most merciful sentence in the whole corpus. Whoever wrote it had sat with the dying." He shook his head, a man acknowledging joinery in a rival house. "We are not the vandals in this story. We are the people who think a thing that powerful should have walls."

"You will want to ask me about Dr. Thorne," he went on, "and I would, in your chair, so let me spare you the asking. I do not know where he is. I am one of a small number of people entitled to say that with authority, and I know how it sounds, and it is true all the same. Nobody in my shop wanted the outcome we have. A missing builder is bad for everyone who builds."

He straightened the saucer a degree.

"Now the unpleasant paragraph, and it is short. Certain session records have gone wandering. Records with the names of serving officers in them, and the layouts of rooms that do not officially hold sessions. I do not know where those records are." He held her eyes, mild as the milk. "Notice that I am not asking. I am telling you the forecast. When that weather breaks, it will break on whoever is standing in the open holding paper. The offer includes a roof. That is everything the paragraph says, and now it is finished, and I apologize for it. It is meant to be one hundred and four again tomorrow, by the way. They are saying the grid will hold."

Somewhere above the stacks the air handling cycled on and ran its long even breath.

"I have one question," Miranda said.

"Please."

"What is your release condition?"

Berel set his cup down.

"Ninety days," he said. "Written notice, either direction. Severance at a year of salary, vesting continues through the notice period, and the non-compete is narrow, it covers client work only, not the field, we are not monsters about it. The title and the collection would stay behind, naturally, but your name stays on the finding aid forever. That is not revocable. I wrote the language myself. Nobody at Cormorant is captive, Miranda. Our people stay twenty and thirty years because there is nowhere else on earth to do the work. If it would settle your mind, I will have employment counsel send you the exit terms before the offer letter, so that you can read the way out before you read the way in."

"Thank you," she said.

He looked at her for a moment. Then he stood, buttoned his jacket, and took the glasses down out of his hair and folded them into a case worn to brass at the corners. He thanked her for the hour. He thanked her, gravely, for the tea, which he had brought.

At the end of the long table he paused.

"The offer stands until events overtake it. Take the five weeks. Take advice. The number on the card is answered at any hour, by a person. And whatever you decide, Ms. Cale, I will hope for continuity. I have enjoyed the conversations this room has given me more than anything else this year."

He let himself out. The reading room door was original to the building, oak over a steel core, hung on a closer that insisted on finishing every arc itself. It sighed. It took its three seconds. It latched.

She sat with the folder squared on her knees while the light went long across the table. The tea kept cooling in both cups. Only one of them was empty.
