#T Provenance

The gift had a paper trail, because gifts to public universities always do. On the Friday of her tenth week Miranda requested the file on her own commission, in writing, on the form, like any other patron, because the discipline of the record does not exempt the one who keeps it.

The gift agreement ran four pages, and the fourth was signatures. The donor of record was The Wetherell Family Foundation, a Delaware nonstock corporation. The purposes clause funded appraisal, stabilization, and continuity of the Thorne deposit, and a rider she had initialed in April without reading hard required weekly memoranda of progress to the Foundation's designated representative. She had sent one every Friday for ten weeks. Inventories, condition notes, queries outstanding, the small dry weather of an appraisal. She had considered the memoranda a courtesy, the sort of thing money is owed. She sat for a while with the word continuity, and then she began to pull filings.

The Wetherell Family Foundation filed what private foundations must file, and what they file is public. Its officers were an attorney in Wilmington, a treasurer with a certified public accountant's license and a Reston address, and a secretary who proved to be the Wilmington attorney again, wearing a middle initial. No Wetherell held any office. No Wetherell had ever held any office. She searched the name and found a mill town in New Hampshire, a headstone database, and a genealogy forum where the line went quiet in 1961. A family foundation with no family is a glove with no hand in it. It keeps the shape of the fingers.

The Foundation's money arrived once a year, in a single contribution, from Anserine Heritage Computing, LLC, also of Wilmington. Heritage computing was not a business she could find a definition for anywhere. It was a phrase built the way a passport photograph is built, to pass, not to be looked at. Anserine had no site, no staff, no telephone. It had a registered agent, and the agent's address was 4501 Weathervane Court, Suite 400, Reston, Virginia, and Suite 400 appeared once more in the record of her afternoon, on the Virginia filing of Cormorant Analytic Group.

Cormorant did not hide. That was the first thing she understood about it, and the understanding never improved. It had a website the color of a good overcoat, and the website said data integration for national security customers, and showed three lobbies photographed in the same brushed light: Reston, Columbia, Austin. The Austin office stood four miles from the reading room she was sitting in. There was a values page, which said stewardship, precision, discretion. There was a careers page, which wanted cleared linguists, corpus operations staff, and something called an estate integration lead. There was no products page. A company with no products page is not concealing its products. It has one customer, and the customer already knows.

She laid the afternoon out on a single sheet, boxes and arrows in her own hand, the way she diagrammed custody for any object with a doubtful past. Foundation, shell, group. Two shells and a bird that fishes. Her fee cleared through three names before it belonged to anyone, and at the bottom of the water it was Cormorant's. She dated the sheet, signed it, and filed it in her working papers, and then she sat with the one fact the diagram could not hold. The gift agreement had been executed nine days after the last day anyone would swear to having seen Jacob Thorne. Nine days is fast for a family foundation. It is not fast for a plan.

Mr. Berel visited the reading room the following Tuesday, in the hour after lunch when the clerestory light goes the color of weak tea. He had no appointment. He signed the register in a small upright hand, G. Berel, and where the form asked for an institution he printed CORMORANT ANALYTIC GROUP in block capitals, unhurried, like a man who had never once needed a lie.

"Miranda," he said, as though they were resuming.

They had never met. He was a neat gray man of about sixty, suit the color of weather about to change, reading glasses on a cord he did not use. He had the stillness of someone who has waited in better rooms than this one and never minded the wait. He requested no materials. The register has a column for materials requested. His is blank.

He stood a polite distance from the trolley of gray boxes and looked at them the way other men look at water from a bridge.

"I have read the public materials," he said. "Many times. The Dossier above all. It is a serious piece of work, whatever one believes about it. People will be reading it in a hundred years, if it has continuity of care."

"Care is what I am here to appraise, Mr. Berel."

"And you appraise it well. Your memoranda are admired. The donor reads them the day they arrive." He said it as a kindness, and it was one, and it was also the whole cargo of the visit, set down gently on the table between them. "It is rare, that quality of attention."

"You found the Foundation thin," he went on, before she had decided what to ask first. "You will forgive it. Foundations are how patience is structured in this country. The tax code has no instrument for continuity, so lawyers build one out of what the code allows. It is improvised, like everything durable."

"What does Cormorant want with a public domain estate?"

"To read it. It is public." He took the glasses off the cord and put them back, a small ceremony that seemed to stand in for a shrug. "Everyone may read it, Miranda. That is the beauty of what he did. He made it so that no one could be stopped."

She watched him look at the boxes again. Whatever he was, he was not pretending the reverence. There is a way a man stands in front of a thing he believes in, weight even, hands still, and Mr. Berel stood that way in a public reading room in front of forty linear feet of a missing man's paper.

"I will use a vulgar phrase," he said, "and you will forgive that too. As an asset class, a whole mind, kept whole, has no comparables. Paintings are shavings from a life. Letters are shavings. This is the tree entire, rings and all, and this is the first century that could keep it. Estates end when the love ends. That is the ordinary rule. The children keep the house and sell the papers, or keep the papers and sell the house, and either way the thing that held it together goes out of the world. My clients do not run out of love, because they do not run out of anything. What they offer the dead is continuity."

Four, she thought. She had been counting without deciding to. It was the only instrument she had brought.

"Conclude whatever you conclude," he said at the door, settling his coat. "Only conclude it thoroughly. He deserves thoroughness." He thanked her for her cooperation, nodded to the boxes as though to a colleague, and went out. She had not, so far as she could tell, cooperated with anything. She understood from the ease of his going that she had, that the cooperation was already banked, and that no receipt would be offered.

After he left she photographed the register page with her phone. She could not have said why.

The fork's rite surfaced two days later, in the ordinary course of the inventory, in the second folder of Thorne's consult papers, between a nondisclosure agreement he had not signed and a visitor badge he had not returned. Six leaves, laser print, no letterhead. The changes were tracked and printed inline, which meant that somewhere a program had been set to preserve every deletion, and someone had chosen that setting, and someone else had let the result out of the building.

She read it standing up. She did not notice she had read it standing up until her knees informed her.

Tracked changes are a genre of honesty. Somewhere in an office with brushed light, a person had been required to record each removal as he made it, and he had done it, in lowercase, without one adjective, and initialed nothing, and the record had held anyway. She had spent ten years learning to trust documents that testify against their own makers, and she trusted this one completely. That was the trouble with it. Nothing in the estate had frightened her. She had said so in the memoranda, with a professional's small pride. The margins frightened her.

She called the associate counsel assigned to the deposit from the office telephone, because her own had begun to feel like a memorandum. It was twenty past four. He listened the way lawyers listen, which is to say audibly.

"Walk me through it once more," he said. "Slower, and with the names in order."

She walked him through it. Foundation, shell, group, suite. The register page. The six leaves and their margins. The silence afterward went on long enough that she checked the display to see whether the call had dropped. It had not.

"The gift is lawful," he said. "Anonymity is ordinary. Universities take quiet money every day and write thank-you letters to a post office box. That is not the part I want you thinking about tonight."

"Which is the part."

"The six leaves. If that document belongs to a government program, it may be classified. If it is classified, this call is already a record of a problem, and I am obligated to people you have not met, and you are obligated to me. Do you understand what I have just said?"

"You said may."

"I did. Write down where every page came from, touch nothing else today, and answer me one thing. Who asked you to trace the gift?"

"No one."

"That is the part I mind," he said, and rang off.

Classified. She entered the word in her day log at twenty to five, in her own hand, because writing a thing down is how you make it hold still. The appraisal became, in that minute, a different kind of object, the way a house becomes a different kind of object when the county learns what is buried under it.

That night she did not diagram anything. She lay in the rented room with the lamp off and the traffic running its long wet sentence down the interstate, and thought about endings, as a professional matter. The estate's rite ended. That was its law and its mercy, and she had catalogued the ending twice, in two forms, grimoire and rite: the voice fades, the vessel dissolves, the mind departs, the understanding stays. In week one she had written that liturgy is a preservation risk, because it invites belief. She had been wrong about where the risk sat. The fork had removed one thing only that mattered, and had explained itself in the margin, in lowercase, in five words. No release condition. Sessions persist.
