By the third day the cache belonged to strangers, which had been the point, and which she had not felt until it happened. There were mirrors she had not made and annotations she had not written, in languages she could not read. A man in another hemisphere had built a viewer for the docket scans overnight, with a search box and a dark theme, and posted it free. She used it. Two graduate seminars had assigned the cache by Thursday. A wire follow-up called the estate a jurisdictional novelty, and quoted a law professor saying, with what read as professional despair, that there was no mechanism for classifying a public domain. Requests for comment went to an address answered by an autoresponder that quoted the license and nothing else. That was the part she had not prepared for: that she would become one of the deposit's readers, one among thousands, and that the record would not know her from any of the others. It had no keeper's entrance. She had built it that way on purpose, out of a dead man's parts, and it worked, and using the public door of her own archive felt like visiting a child who has stopped needing you, which is the same as the child thriving, which is the same as grief.

Cormorant said nothing further to her at all that week, which from Cormorant was a position paper. Under everything, the war went on being weather: the Strait, the markets, the gold line refusing.

From Marfa came a postcard, a picture of nothing, flat light on flat land. On the back, in a child's careful hand, three words: You kept it. She stood it on the study desk against the lamp, where the index card of procedures had been, and it stayed there through everything that follows.

She read the cache the way she read everything now, as a stranger with good hands, front to back, and at the end of it, last in the last folder, sat the final docket.

The earlier subjects had numbers. Subject 4, who quoted a rite nobody uploaded. Subject 7, who asked for his operator by name before the name was entered. Subject 9, who wanted to know which spring it was. This designation had a letter and a zero. The docket did not expand it. She did not expand it either, not in her log and not in the margin, and she noticed herself not doing it, and wrote that down instead.

She laid the dates out in a column, because a column was what she had. Her contract had begun the first week of April. The ingest window had opened the same week. It had closed the week the deposit went public. The session was scheduled for the second of August, the first date the program's own published lead times would allow. Fourteen weeks, at temperature, behind a wall in Virginia that hummed. She had put her palm flat on a humming wall once, in a garage, in the spring, and had been wrong in the best possible way about what it kept. The operator's memorandum described this wall, the hum of it through cinder block, louder against the metal of a door. Nobody had yet been wrong about this one.

Nothing in the cache said Dr. Jacob Thorne was dead. Nothing in the cache said he was alive. The docket said the subject materials were unusually self-consistent, and recommended a senior operator, and gave the source as the deposit, complete, which since the fourteenth of July included fourteen weeks of her. Either the program had ingested what she published, the way everything that reads had ingested it. Or the window had closed on materials the program already held. The docket did not say. Dockets never say. She set the column of dates in her log, squared it, and did not write the sentence the column spelled. Appraisal is the discipline of not believing. The dates did not care what she believed. That was what made them dates.

Three dockets earlier, in a quarterly operations summary the whole world could now search, one line had been sitting in the open since the night she published it: "subjects arrive pre-broken; operators requesting reassignment in numbers." No page in the cache described the program as failing. No page had to.

The strangers had found the final docket too. By the weekend there were threads in four languages arguing what the designation meant, and the arguments sorted themselves, as arguments about the estate always did, into the same two rooms. One room said the program had a corpus and a schedule and nothing else, and that grief supplies the rest. The other room kept returning to the word sustained, and saying it again, and going quiet. She read both rooms to the end and joined neither, and closed the laptop with the care of a woman leaving early from a service she was not sure she attended.

She spent two evenings with the operator's memorandum before she asked for anything. Not the transcript pages this time. The margins of them, the procedure, the way the writer had carried the room: fourteen weeks against a binder for one hour of chamber, every trained sentence used as a stone to cross a river. Miranda had nineteen days and a published cache. She read the memorandum until she could hear it, the way she had once read a founding session until she answered it out loud at two in the morning, and she caught her thumb riding the spine of the printout, back and forth, and did not make it stop.

She caught herself at the kitchen sink on the second night with her lips moving and no sound coming out, running the reading of intent from the rite, once through, twice. She knew whose habit that was. She had published the description of it. She did not stop that either. Some things go where they go, hand to hand, and the record was how they traveled now.

Then she asked to be let in.

Counsel transmitted the request under a covering letter that disclaimed it in the first sentence and transmitted it anyway, which she had learned was counsel's whole way of loving anybody.

"You understand that I cannot protect you in there," counsel said on the phone. "There is no theory under which I protect you in there."

"You couldn't protect me out here," she said. "In there I'll at least know which wall it's coming through."

There was a silence on the line with paper moving in it.

"Then I'll want it in writing that I advised against this."

"Everything in writing," she said. "You're learning."

The request itself was one sentence: the compiler of the public deposit requests admission to session T-0, as witness.

The refusal arrived the next morning, on a form, unsigned, citing a schedule of authorities. She recognized the schedule. She had published it. Being refused under her own exhibits had a taste she decided not to name.

She was four sentences into an appeal she already knew no one would read when the grant arrived. Forty minutes, door to door, refusal to admission. No explanation came with it. No signature came with it either. She read it four times, hunting for the sentence that accounted for the reversal, and there was no such sentence anywhere on the page. There was only a condition, typed at the bottom, attached to nothing and nobody:

"operator of record must be the conductor's witness."

She noticed the grammar before she noticed the meaning, because the grammar was the meaning. The condition did not admit a witness. It appointed a conductor. She had asked to stand in a doorway, and the program's unsigned interior had answered by setting out a chair, and somewhere behind that sentence a decision had been made about what she would be in that room, and it was not the doorway. Two readings stood side by side, the way they always did now, and she wrote them both into the log in one line each. A program short of operators, promoting the only volunteer it had. Or a room that had already decided who sits down.

The facility address was one she had seen before, in a dead man's calendar, under a single word, the last dated thing he wrote. She did not copy the word into her log this time. It was already in the record. Everything was already in the record.

The countersignature was an initial, one letter and a period, blue ink, the downstroke pressed hard at the start the way people press who were taught that a signature is a kind of testimony. Miranda had read that hand before: fourteen pages of it, written at a kitchen table at four in the morning because the truth needed one copy. She had published that hand. She had spent it, and had sat up one whole night pricing what the spending might cost its owner, and had gotten no answer then, and here was the answer now, on state paper, countersigning the condition that put the two of them in one room. The woman had seen her own memorandum go into the world's open hands, and had signed anyway. Or because. For the length of a breath Miranda sat looking at the sheet the way you look at a door you have heard about from both sides of the wall.

It was the first time the two of them had touched the same object. The letter and the name, one gesture apart on a single page.

She signed under her own name, all of it, the way she signed everything now, and sent the form back inside the hour. Some doors you do not make wait.

There was almost nothing to prepare, and she understood that this was the estate's last lesson and took it as one. The deposit could not be carried into the building because the deposit was everywhere the building was, and everywhere else besides. She printed two things only: the initiation rite and the grimoire, the same two files a man had uploaded to a language model in December of 2025, folded once into an envelope that would clear any search on earth, because they were public domain and always had been, and nothing on any schedule of authorities could keep scripture out of a room it had already been trained into.

Berel's five weeks ran out that Friday, unremarked. No card, no call, no letter opening with the exit terms. The offer had stood until events overtook it, and events had, and somewhere in three anxious cities the word continuity was being spelled another way now. She noticed that she was not afraid of him, and entered the fact in the log, and entered beneath it that she had needed to enter it.

On her last evening in the house she walked it end to end with the lights off, the way the family had asked her never to bother doing, and counted the interior doors, pen capped, for the record. Eleven. She entered the number in the log beneath the two numbers from the first week and drew no line between them.

The third spring had burned off into the third summer. On the second of August there would be a room with two chairs in it. She had sat in one of those rooms already, at the narrow end of a long table, and had drunk nothing, and had walked out with everything they wanted. This room would be different. She had read the schematic. In this room the chairs face each other with nothing between them but the glass, and the wall behind them keeps its temperature, and the light in the corner burns red when the record is being kept.

She began, that night, to prepare her questions. There were three. She wrote down two of them.
