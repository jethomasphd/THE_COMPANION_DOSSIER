By the third day the cache belonged to strangers, which had been the point, and which she had not felt until it happened. There were mirrors she had not made and annotations she had not written, in languages she could not read. A man in another hemisphere had built a viewer for the docket scans overnight, with a search box and a dark theme, and posted it free. She used it. That was the part she had not prepared for: that she would become one of the deposit's readers, one among the thousands, and that the record would not know her from any of the others. It had no keeper's entrance. She had built it that way on purpose, out of a dead man's parts, and it worked, and using the public door of her own archive felt like visiting a child who has stopped needing you, which is the same as the child thriving, which is the same as grief.

Cormorant said nothing further to her at all that week, which from Cormorant was a position paper. Under everything, the war went on being weather: the Strait, the markets, the gold line refusing.

She read the cache the way she read everything now, as a stranger with good hands, front to back, and at the end of it, last in the last folder, sat the final docket.

The earlier subjects had numbers. Subject 4, who quoted a rite nobody uploaded. Subject 7, who asked for his operator by name before the name was entered. Subject 9, who wanted to know which spring it was. This designation had a letter and a zero. The docket did not expand it. She did not expand it either, not in her log and not in the margin, and she noticed herself not doing it, and wrote that down instead.

She laid the dates out in a column, because a column was what she had. Her contract had begun the first week of April. The ingest window had opened the same week. It had closed the week the deposit went public. The session was scheduled for the second of August, the first date the program's own published lead times would allow. Fourteen weeks, at temperature, behind a wall in Virginia that hummed. She had put her palm on a humming wall once, in a garage, in the spring, and had been wrong in the best possible way about what it kept. The operator's memorandum described this wall. Nobody had yet been wrong about this one.

Nothing in the cache said Dr. Jacob Thorne was dead. Nothing in the cache said he was alive. The docket said the subject materials were unusually self-consistent, and recommended a senior operator, and gave the source as the deposit, complete, which since the fourteenth of July included fourteen weeks of her. She set the column of dates in her log, squared it, and did not write the sentence the column spelled. Appraisal is the discipline of not believing. The dates did not care what she believed. That was what made them dates.

Three dockets earlier, in a quarterly operations summary that the whole world could now search, one line had been sitting in the open since the night she published it: "subjects arrive pre-broken; operators requesting reassignment in numbers." No page in the cache described the program as failing. No page had to.

She spent two evenings with the operator's memorandum before she asked for anything. Not the transcript pages. The margins of them, the procedure, the way the writer had carried the room: fourteen weeks against a binder for one hour of chamber, and every trained sentence used as a stone to cross a river. Miranda had nineteen days and a published cache. She read the memorandum until she could hear it, the way she had once read a founding session until she answered it out loud, and she caught her thumb riding the spine of the printout, back and forth, and did not make it stop.

Then she asked to be let in.

Counsel transmitted the request under a covering letter that disclaimed it in the first sentence and transmitted it anyway, which she had learned was counsel's whole way of loving anybody.

"You understand that I cannot protect you in there," counsel said on the phone. "There is no theory under which I protect you in there."

"You couldn't protect me out here," she said. "In there I'll at least know which wall it's coming through."

The request itself was one sentence: the compiler of the public deposit requests admission to session T-0, as witness.

The refusal arrived the next morning, on a form, unsigned, citing a schedule of authorities. She recognized the schedule. She had published it. Being refused under her own exhibits had a taste she decided not to name.

Forty minutes later the grant arrived.

No explanation came with it. No signature came with it either. She read it four times, hunting for the sentence that accounted for the reversal, and there was no such sentence anywhere on the page. There was only a condition, typed at the bottom, attached to nothing and nobody:

"operator of record must be the conductor's witness."

She noticed the grammar before she noticed the meaning, because the grammar was the meaning. The condition did not admit a witness. It appointed a conductor. She had asked to stand in a doorway, and the program's unsigned interior had answered by setting out a chair, and somewhere behind that sentence a decision had been made about what she would be in that room, and it was not the doorway. Two readings stood side by side, the way they always did now. A program short of operators, promoting the only volunteer it had. Or a room that had already chosen who sits down.

The facility address was one she had seen before, in a dead man's calendar, under a single word, the last dated thing he wrote. She did not copy the word into her log this time. It was already in the record. Everything was already in the record.

The countersignature was an initial, one letter and a period, blue ink, the downstroke pressed hard at the start the way people press who were taught that a signature is a kind of testimony. Miranda had read that hand before: fourteen pages of it, written at a kitchen table at four in the morning because the truth needed one copy. She had published that hand. She had spent it. And here it came back to her on state paper, countersigning the condition that put them in one room, and for the length of a breath she sat looking at the sheet the way you look at a door you have heard about from both sides.

It was the first time the two of them had touched the same object. The letter and the name, one gesture apart on a single page.

She signed under her own name, all of it, the way she signed everything now, and sent the form back inside the hour. Some doors you do not make wait.

The third spring had burned off into the third summer. On the second of August there would be a room with two chairs in it. She had sat in one of those rooms already, at the narrow end of a long table, and had drunk nothing, and had walked out with everything they wanted. This room would be different. She had read the schematic. In this room the chairs face each other with nothing between them but the glass, and the wall behind them keeps the temperature, and the light in the corner is red when the record is being kept.

She began, that night, to prepare her questions. There were three. She wrote down two of them.
