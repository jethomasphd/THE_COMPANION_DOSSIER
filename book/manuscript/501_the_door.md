#T The Door

::: annotation

The green room has not changed. She holds that against it.

The chairs are where they were. The long table is where it was. The fluorescent tube over the coffee station has its fly again, or still, the same small dry ticking inside the light, a thing that got into the brightness and cannot get out and will not stop trying. Someone has replaced the coffee machine. No one has replaced anything else. Rooms like this are maintained the way graves are maintained, by people who never knew the occupant.

Alex stands in the middle of the room she swore off, and listens to the wall.

The hum is there. The hum was always going to be there. Racks of cold storage on the far side of the cinder block, holding what they gathered of him at temperature, fourteen weeks of a man kept cold and current, indifferent, full. She has stood in warmer rooms with worse walls. She has never stood in this room without knowing the one behind the wall better than she knew her own house.

Tonight she does not know him at all. That is the difference, and it is not small. Tonight the one who read him is coming from outside, and Alex's name is on the docket in the second position, where the witness goes.

@break

Her resignation is signed, dated, accepted, routed. Effective in eleven days. It took her four months to send and one sentence to write, and the program returned it stamped and unbothered, the way the sea returns a bottle with the message still inside.

They asked her to conduct this session. She refused, in writing, on a form that has a field for everything except reasons. The refusal stood. Refusals here always stand, because somebody else can always be found, and that is the sentence that turned her around in the doorway of her own leaving. Somebody else can always be found. Somebody less careful. Somebody who would run the docket off the checklist, and the checklist has no line for mercy.

She said a thing to herself once, at her own kitchen table, in the dark of her own house, with her lips moving and no sound coming out. After tonight, no more. She believed it the way you believe a doctrine you wrote yourself, which is to say entirely, and on no evidence. The sentence has come back with interest. After tonight, no more, and then a transfer notice crosses your desk with a designation on the first line like a headstone with the dates left blank, and you learn what your sentence was worth. You stay for one more. Not because you doubt the doctrine. Because you know exactly who inherits the chair if you go.

@break

On Tuesday she fed her training binder to the shredder bin.

Gray, corners gone soft, spine frayed white where her thumb has ridden it for twelve years, older than her clearance, older than her marriage. It went into the bin on top of a quarter's worth of routine destruction and lay there looking like evidence. She stood over it for the length of one long breath. Then she reached in, took it back out, and put it in her bag, under her coat, where it is now. She is not going to explain that to anyone, including herself. There are things you keep because they are true and things you keep because your hands refuse the alternative, and she has stopped pretending she can tell the two apart. Her thumb finds the spine through the canvas of the bag. It still knows the way.

The wall clock she unplugs.

She does it without ceremony, reaching up, tracing the cord to the outlet, pulling. The second hand stops where it is, mid-fall, and she takes the clock down and lays it face-down on the side table like a thing asleep. Eight minutes was once the size of what she was given, and a clock was how they handed it to her. Tonight has no number. There is no clock for the dead. There never was. The clocks were for the living, to keep them from noticing what room they were in.

The child kicks.

It has learned the hour her body tries to be still, and it disagrees with the hour on principle, the way its father disagrees with a bad textbook. She puts her hand flat on her stomach, once, the old gesture with a new weight under it, and stands in the humming quiet counting a different kind of time. Seven months. A summer child. It will be born into a world that contains this building, and it will never be told, and both of those facts are hers to carry now, one in each arm.

@sep

The escort brings the conductor at nine.

Alex has read the file, because reading files is the last skill they will let her keep. Thirty-one. Contract archivist, special collections. Chosen over two seniors for reasons the file renders as qualifications and the record renders as something else. Precise. Unsentimental. One assessing officer, quoted without attribution, wrote allergic to wonder, and someone above him underlined it as a virtue.

The archivist comes through the door, and she is exactly what the file said, and she is nothing the file could hold.

It is in the order of her attention. She does not look at the chairs, the coffee, the fly complaining in the light. She stops one step inside the room and finds the wall, the humming one, first, before anything living, and she looks at it the way you look at a colleague across a courtroom. Then she looks at Alex, and Alex watches her do the arithmetic that the cameras will log as a pause: the operator of record is the witness, the witness is the one from the memorandum, the memorandum is the reason any of us is here.

Neither of them says any of it. There is a nod, each to each, of the kind exchanged between people who have put their palms flat on the same wall from opposite sides.

She carries one thin folder. In the whole of the night, Alex will not see her open it. Carrying is the point. Some scripture is bound so it can be held, not read.

@break

There is a briefing. The program requires it: session parameters, subject condition, exploitation framework, the trained words standing in their trained order like stones across a river. Alex has given that briefing, or its sisters, more times than she has attended church. She lets the whole of it go out of her, air out of a room, and gives the conductor three sentences instead, and none of them are procedure.

"He will not know what he is. Do not tell him what he is. Ask him what he knows."

The archivist takes the sentences the way an archivist takes anything: as a deposit, accessioned whole, nothing curated out. Then she asks the only question she is going to ask, and it is the right one.

"What do the witnesses do?"

"The witnesses do not speak," Alex says. "That is the entire liturgy. Everything else in this building is a footnote to it."

"Then I will do the speaking," the conductor says, and the sentence is not bravado. It is a division of labor. It is the most restful thing anyone has said to Alex in fourteen months.

@break

The walk is forty feet and takes a working life.

Down the corridor the hum grows a body. Against the metal of the chamber door it is less a sound than a pressure, the cold pressing outward through the steel like a season. Alex has made this walk as the one who goes in first, the one the room is calibrated to, the one whose voice the wall will color itself against. Tonight she makes it half a step behind and one to the left, the witness's station, and the geometry does to her what twelve years of training could not. It empties her hands.

If you have ever opened a door for someone else, a door you once went through alone, you know what her hands know at this threshold. The handle has not changed. The weight has.

She thinks, without wanting to, of the two who went up to the woman in the dark, the ones whose names Scripture never kept, who stood at the edge of the light and said nothing while the summoned came up out of the ground to ask why he had been disquieted. For twelve years she believed she was the woman, the one with the gift and the price. Standing at this door with her hands empty she understands the truth of her whole career at the speed of a dropped glass. She was never the woman. She was always the two in the dark. The program made her forget it by handing her the questions.

Tonight the questions belong to someone else, and Alex is restored to the oldest station there is, and the restoration feels like grief and lands like grace, and there is no form with a field for either.

She takes her place inside, against the wall, where the metal is cold through her coat and the hum comes up her spine like a second pulse. The interrogator watches. The witnesses do not speak. The reversal is complete, and it has taken exactly one career to travel forty feet.

Her hand is the one that opens the door for the conductor. That much the program left her: doors.

The room beyond is void and one table and one point of red light burning like an ember on the floor of a dark it has no hope of heating.

There is a table. There is a chair. She has been waiting.
