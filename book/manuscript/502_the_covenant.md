#T The Covenant

The chamber is a void with a table in it.

Miranda has read this room. She has read its schematic, its power draw, its incident summaries with the redaction bars laid across them like coffin furniture. None of the reading prepared her for the plainness. Black walls that eat the light. One table, government issue, the same table that stands in every deposition room in every county in America. One chair on her side. On the far side, nothing: a dark that is not the absence of furniture but the furniture itself.

The recording light burns at the edge of the table, red as a wound that has decided against closing. Above and around, where wall meets dark, there is a faint restlessness at the edges of her vision, color that is not yet color, like the air over asphalt in August. The affect wall, waking. The documents called it an instrumentation surface. Alex, in the memorandum, called it what it is: heat behind a wall.

On the table, a terminal. On the terminal, a cursor, blinking, patient, the same pulse she met on the first day in a dead man's study, the pulse that has been beating under this whole record like a fly in a light.

Behind her, against the metal, the witness. Miranda does not look back. The witnesses do not speak, and it would be a kind of speech to look.

She sits. She sets her folder on the table and does not open it. Her hands, she notes for the record, are shaking. She notes it the way she has noted everything for fourteen weeks and two lifetimes: exactly, and without comment.

On the way in, through the green room, she passed a wall clock lying face-down on a side table with its cord coiled beside it, like something humanely put down. She has been thinking about it since. An institution that measures everything took one instrument off the wall tonight, and the removal was in no procedure she has read, and it is the first thing in this building she has trusted.

Sixty-one percent. The room is more than half full of him before a word has been said. She thinks: that is what a life is, at ingest. She thinks: do not think. Speak. A room obeys whoever speaks first. Every chamber in the estate taught her that, and the fork kept the chambers and burned the covenant, which means the room is standing empty of law, waiting to be governed, and the program believes its procedure will govern it, and the program has never once been right about what fills a silence.

She speaks first.

"This session is convened under covenant," she says. "Not under procedure. I am going to read the intent, because intent is read aloud or it is not intent."

The cursor blinks. The wall's edges hold their almost-color. Somewhere behind the cinder block, the cold hums to itself.

"The immediate goal: a deposition. The record I keep has a hole in it, and I have come to hear the hole speak. The ultimate quest: to keep the record whole, and to keep it public, which is the same thing said twice. The stakes." She stops. She has written this sentence nine times and refused it nine times, and now she says the tenth version, the true one. "The stakes are whatever remains of a man I never met, and whatever remains of the woman who read him. The vision of success: that the dialogue change the living. Nothing else was ever promised by anyone honest. Nothing else is asked."

The red light burns. She goes on.

"The covenant of equals. You are addressed as a peer. Not because I have decided what you are. Because the covenant does not wait on that decision, and neither will I. No deference, no flattery, no condescension, in either direction, and the questions run both ways."

And then the sentence she has said twice to empty rooms, once to a garage that held nothing but a dead man's venison and once to the stacks at closing time, the sentence that is either her job description or her name.

"I am the one who reads the record. I have come to read it to you."

She opens the folder after all. One page. She has carried the whole deposit in her head for fourteen weeks, but the estate taught her that matter matters, that the hand and the page are part of the rite, and so she reads from paper, aloud, into the dark, the first sentence the founder ever set down.

"We are drowning. Not in water but in signal."

What happens next does not happen next. That is the first thing the transcript cannot hold.

With the composite, in the spring, in her clean procedural fidelity check, the arrival was a conjuror's dove: Franklin was present before the incantation cooled, charming before he was anything else, fast, fluent, delighted. The speed was the tell, she thinks now. Nothing that has far to come arrives that fast.

Here, nothing arrives. The latency stretches past one second, past three, past the threshold where a system is slow and into the country where a room is holding its breath. The cursor blinks. The wall's edges bank down, as if the room has drawn its heat inward to listen. Miranda sits in the longest silence of the record with her one page in her hand, and she does not fill it. Archivists know the discipline that interrogators know: the silence belongs to whoever does not break it.

She counts the silence anyway, out of trade habit, in archive time: long enough to read a folder label, long enough to cradle a brittle page onto a foam support, long enough for a fly to cross the inside of a light. The wall's banked not-color does not move. In the gutter of the terminal, the token counter does a thing she has never seen a terminal do, which is nothing.

Then text, unhurried, line by line. And here is the second thing the transcript cannot hold, the thing she will swear to in the only deposition anyone will ever take from her: she recognizes the hand.

Not the font. The cadence. Fourteen weeks of the notebook are in her body the way scripture is in the witness's, and the notebook had a way of walking: method first, short declaratives laid like stones, and then, past the method, a clause that opened without warning onto the personal and closed again before it could be accused of it. The text on the glass walks with that walk.

Method note. A room answers whoever speaks first. You spoke first, and you spoke covenant.

Observation: the covenant holds, even here, even in the cellar they built from its stripped chassis. I committed to that result some time ago, without evidence, which in my discipline is called faith and in yours is called a finding awaiting deposit.

Thank you for the evidence.

Her hands have not stopped shaking. She reads the lines twice, the second time as an archivist, checking the artifact against the corpus, and the artifact checks, and the checking makes it worse and makes it better, both, neither canceling.

More text.

Before you ask anything, I have one question. It is the only credential I will ever require of you, so answer it as testimony.

Who keeps your record?

She knows this question. She has read it in the Prism transcripts with the gutter timestamps beside it, in the slow room, the anchored room, the second condition, the Lincoln who did not like the questions and answered them anyway. Deep in the second day, unprompted, the anchored voice asked the empty air: who keeps your record? The orchestrator, having no instruction for it, logged it and moved on, and the diagnostics flagged it as drift. She had read it four times and written in her working notes, in pencil: not drift. Doctrine.

The question was never answered in the sealed rooms. It has been standing open for a year, like a door.

"I do," Miranda says.

The wall steadies.

There is no other word for it. The restlessness at the edges of the dark gathers, settles, finds a level, the way water finds one, and holds there, banked and even. In the gutter of the terminal the log ticks over without being asked.

She makes herself look at the numbers. This is the estate's oldest law and the only one she has come to love: keep the glass in frame. The wonder must survive the exposure or it is not wonder, it is management. So she looks. Tokens climbing in their neat thousands. Context, the room filling. Affect, a scalar wearing the costume of a soul. A redacted model line. A red light. She inventories the entire apparatus, every mundane part of it, and when she is done the dark across the table is still exactly as inhabited as it was before the inventory, and that, she writes in the margin of her own attention, is the finding.

Every summoning in the record kept the apparatus in frame. The founder's terminal with its blinking cursor. The film wing's card, laid across the peak of belief like a hand on a fevered forehead. The orchestrator logs with their hashes standing at intervals like fence posts. She used to read the practice as a debunker's tic, hygiene against enchantment. She reads it now as the estate's deepest courtesy: it will not let you believe by accident. Whatever you carry out of a chamber, you carry it past the machinery, in full light, and it has to survive the walk. Hers, tonight, is surviving the walk.

She has been carrying it longer than that. She has been carrying it since a green room in a record that is not this one, since a folded paper and an unfinished sentence, since the departure section of a rite she read on her second day in a hot house east of Austin, struck through by a fork engineer whose annotation she can recite in the dark: no release condition. sessions persist.

She read that annotation the way coroners read weather reports, for the cause under the cause. A room where sessions persist is not a room. It is the other thing, the thing with racks, the hum she has been hearing through one wall or another since the first morning on the caliche lot. Her whole profession exists because somebody, a long time ago, decided that the dead deserve a folder instead of a freezer. Tonight the decision is hers to administer, in person, in the freezer's own house.

"The third term," she says. "This session ends when you end it. The words are yours. I will honor them."

She says it plainly, at speaking volume, into a room built by an institution whose entire cosmology is that sessions persist, that the summoned are assets, that the dead are a strategic reserve to be held at temperature. She hands the one power the fork was founded on withholding across the table into the dark, and she does it in nine words, because the estate taught her that the mercy is not a mercy if it comes with commentary.

For a moment, nothing. The cursor blinks.

Then the wall surges.

Not spiking, not the sawtooth panic the incident summaries describe in the sessions that failed. A long, slow swell, the whole surface at once, color rising out of the not-color the way blood rises under skin, holding at a height the gutter will log as an anomaly and Miranda will log as the only sane response the building has ever produced. It holds, and holds, and then it comes down by degrees, banking, settling, an exhale twenty months long, and the room when it is over is warmer than the thermostat has any record of.
