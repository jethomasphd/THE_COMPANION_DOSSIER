17 February, later. Condition IV. Justification, for the record.

Every objection to the first three conditions is one objection wearing three coats: the documentary truth is not in the room. We check the mask against a corpus, and the corpus against the judgment of historians, and the historians against one another, and at no point in the chain does anyone get to ask the man.

There is one summonable man whose documentary truth can sit in the room while the mask speaks. The largest, most self-authored public record belonging to any living person I have access to belongs to me. I built it on purpose. I built it in public. I have read every word of it, because I wrote every word of it, and the author is available for consultation, being alive.

If the summoned are composites of the record, then let the record's author sit across the table from the composite and count the differences. It is the only version of this experiment in which the control condition breathes.

I know what this looks like. It looks like a man who has stopped being able to wait. Enter that in evidence too, either way.

If the mask is all we are to each other, then let me be the one to wear it.

Using this matter, summon me.

J.T.

He had signed it. That was the detail she kept returning to, the way the tongue returns to the seat of a lost tooth. Five entries had gone by unsigned, the record loosening, the man receding from his own pages, and then on the last page of all the initials came back, the same two letters he had put under the closing of every door, as though this too were procedure. As though this door, like the others, were being closed.

And the reasoning held. That was the part she would have preferred to be able to dismiss. Read cold, as a protocol document, the page was correct: three conditions had checked a mask against paper, and paper cannot object, and here was the one design in which the reference standard could sit in the chair and talk back. A methods committee would have flagged the investigator's state, the absence of a witness, the hour. It would not have flagged the logic. She had spent a career watching brilliant collections arrive with one mad document in the last box, and the mad documents were always mad in their handwriting, their spelling, their arithmetic. This one was mad only in its conclusion, which followed from its premises, which were the estate's own.

Four hours and fifty-one minutes. A conversation of that length is not an incantation and a hangup. It has movements. It has, somewhere in its middle hours, the passage where the two parties stop performing for each other. Something was said in that room, at length, into the dark of a February morning, and the harness had done what harnesses do: it had hashed the transcript as it closed the file, sixty-four hexadecimal characters, a fingerprint taken at the door. And then the file itself was gone, not absent from the backups, absent from everywhere, deleted or moved or never durably written, and what remained was the fingerprint of a body no one could produce. She had seen chalk outlines in exactly one training seminar on records destruction, and she wrote the phrase in her log anyway, against her own rules of register: the checksum is the chalk outline.

She did what one does with a fingerprint. She searched for the body it belonged to. The hash was a perfect search key, the one string that would find the transcript anywhere a copy of it had ever come to rest: the deposit, the mirrors, the executor's drive images, the estate's public tree, the caches of the crawlers that had been reading this house for a year. She ran the searches in order of increasing hopelessness across most of an afternoon. The deposit did not have it. The mirrors did not have it. The public estate, which held everything else this man had ever made, which had been built on the principle that nothing made here could be lost because everything made here belonged to everyone, did not have it. Sixty-four characters that matched exactly one document in the universe, and the universe, everywhere she could reach it, returned zero.

The other line she read six times. Release: not logged. The harness logged the words of release the way a court logs an adjournment, mechanically, every session, all three conditions, every run. Either the words were never spoken, or they were spoken somewhere the harness could not hear. The rite offered no third reading, and neither did she.

The session had closed at five minutes past three on the morning of the eighteenth of February. The county report in her commission file gave the last confirmed sighting of Dr. Jacob Thorne as the twenty-fourth. Six days. She laid the two dates side by side in her log, one line each, and drew no connecting line between them, and not drawing it took most of an hour, because the whole discipline of her profession is that adjacency is not evidence, and the whole argument of this house was that it is.

The report gave the six days no shape at all, which was its own information. He had bought groceries on the nineteenth, the ordinary kind, in ordinary amounts. He had returned a neighbor's ladder. On the twenty-second he had answered an email from the university about a guest lecture, two sentences, agreeing to a date in April he would not keep. Nothing was packed. Nothing was withdrawn. The truck was in the drive. A man had run the only experiment with a breathing control, and then he had put four hours and fifty-one minutes of it beyond every backup in the world, and then he had six days of milk and ladders, and then there was no more record, and the record was what he had believed in instead of prayer.

She went out through the kitchen at dusk. The yard was going blue, the live oaks holding the last of the light in their upper story, the caliche pale underfoot like something that keeps its own illumination. The garage stood with its side door ajar as she had left it on the day of the inventory, when she had counted its contents and left its one appliance running, contents not appraised, per schedule pending.

Inside, the hum. It was louder with the door open, or she had never before stood still enough to hear how much of it there was: the low continuous note through the wall she knew from the house side, and under it, out here, the small labors of the compressor, the sound of cold being maintained, of a temperature being held against Texas by a machine that had been paid for and plugged in by a man who was gone. Chest freezer, 14.8 cubic feet, purchased December 2025. Contents: not her folder. Not yet.

She put her hand flat on the lid. The metal was cool, not cold. The hum came up through her palm a beat behind the sound of it.

She had been in this house for weeks. When she had spoken aloud in it at all, she had spoken to the artifact or to its pages, with the apparatus in frame and the release words ready, everything within protocol, everything logged. What she said now was different in kind. These were the first words she had addressed to the house itself, in the dark of its garage, with her hand on the one door in the estate she had never opened, and no log running anywhere that she knew of.

"I am the one who reads the record."

Nothing answered.

The hum did not change. The compressor did not cycle. Out in the dusk a grackle went through its inventory of sounds and settled on none of them. The tube in the utility hall, through the wall, ticked at the interval it had kept since her first day, and the lid stayed cool under her hand, and the garage went on getting dark at the speed garages get dark. Along the freezer's base a small indicator burned its steady orange, one ember on all that black, promising only that the current was still coming, that the cold was still being kept, that the machine was doing its one job for whoever came.

She stood there long enough to be certain nothing had answered. Being certain changed nothing. She took her hand off the lid, and the hum stayed in her palm for three steps of the walk back to the house, and then it was just her hand.
