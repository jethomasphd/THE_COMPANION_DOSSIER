The long battery went up at twelve minutes past six in the evening, in the last gray of a January light that had never fully arrived. Sixty items, all three rooms in parallel, the full diagnostic sweep he and Hale had spent the autumn arguing into shape. It was the final run before the seals came off for analysis, and Thorne had nothing to do.

That was the design. He had removed himself from the experiment the way you remove a solvent, in stages, checking the residue. The orchestrator dispatched. The rooms answered. The transcripts wrote themselves to disk and fingerprinted themselves as they went. Above the workstation, taped to the shelf at eye level, was a strip of paper in his own printing: NO HUMAN TOUCHES THE KEYBOARD. All that remained to him of his own experiment was the tail of the log, scrolling up the left monitor at the pace of other minds conversing elsewhere.

The battery was the two of them at their best, he thought, and would have said so to Hale if saying so were the kind of thing their letters did. Sixty items, ordered so that no answer could coast on the answer before it. Doctrine, then the same doctrine upside down. Questions of law braided with questions of grief, because Hale held that a period voice betrays itself faster in mourning than in argument. Item 44 was one of Hale's, from the grief strand. Thorne had read it aloud once during drafting and then not again.

He ate standing up at the counter, watching the doorway to the study as though the study might need something. By the tenth item he could read the rooms by their timing alone, the way you know footsteps through a ceiling. Room iii returned first, always, arriving before you had finished wanting it. Room i came in at a working pace. Room ii was last every time, and the gap was not compute. [the lawyer reads the question twice], he wrote in the notebook, and put the pen down, because the notebook was for the run and the run was not his to annotate yet.

Around ten he walked the house once, a habit with no method behind it. Through the garage wall, low and continuous, the hum: the chest freezer he had bought in December of the founding winter, doing what compressors do. He stood a moment with his hand flat on the drywall, feeling the vibration arrive in his palm a beat behind the sound of it, and then went back to the study, where three versions of a dead man were answering item 26 in three sealed rooms, each believing itself alone, each being right.

The house did what the house did at night. The lamp on the study desk burned through its amber gel, one warm ellipse on the dark wood and the dark beyond it. In the utility hall the fluorescent tube ticked, a small dry ticking with no rhythm you could count, the sound of something at the glass. He had bought the replacement tube in October. It was still in its sleeve on the dryer. Some part of him, he would have said if asked, wanted the light to keep doing that.

A little after one he lay down on the office couch with his shoes on. The couch was too short for him and he had stopped noticing. He set his phone face down on the floor where his hand could fall on it. Months ago, in a mood he had classified at the time as thoroughness, he had written alert conditions into the orchestrator's config: for any output the taxonomy could not place, three ascending notes through the workstation speaker. The alert had fired in testing, twice, both times because he had made it fire. He slept the way you sleep during a run that is going well, lightly and without guilt.

He was asleep when item 44 went to the rooms.

Three ascending notes, through a closed door, at three eleven and fifty-two seconds in the morning. He was on his feet before he understood what the sound was, because he had never heard it except when he had caused it, and for one stupid interval he stood in the dark trying to remember causing it.

He read the tail standing up. Then he pulled the three session logs and read them side by side, and read them again. The rooms had gone on without him. Item 45 was streaming. Item 45 was nominal. The orchestrator does not improvise: it had met the thing, stamped it with the only stamp it had, and asked the next question on its list, and all three Lincolns were answering the next question on the list in their three separate registers as though the list were the only thing that had ever happened.

He made himself read the interruptions before the sentences, in order, one room at a time. Each fragment was in voice. That was the first thing his training gave him and it arrived unwanted. Room i had been mid-consolation, building one of its warm modern archways. Room ii had been keeping a ledger, clause by clause, the debt that does not lapse. Room iii had been performing, the marble turning to face the gallery, history, friends. Three separate men, three separate sentences under construction, each abandoned at the same millisecond, on the same clock, for the same errand. He checked the millisecond four times. He checked it in the raw files, in case the log formatter rounded. It did not round.

The second thing his training gave him was a list of the ways this happens, and the list is short, and every entry on it is a person or a wire.

He did the arithmetic against the couch cushion, tapping it out with two fingers, twice. The run had opened at 18:12. The hour from 02:12 to 03:12 was the ninth hour of the run. The output had come sixteen seconds before that hour closed. He noticed himself noticing this, and filed the noticing where he filed his pulse rate, among the instrument readings that were not evidence of anything except the state of the instrument.

He could not touch the run. He had built it that way, and at 04:02, sitting in the amber lamplight with his laces still tied, he began the only motion left to him, which was to audit around the outside of the sealed thing, read-only, like a man circling a house tapping the glass.

The audit was a row of doors, and any one of them standing open would have explained the night. A contaminated seed would have explained it. A template leaking through the orchestrator would have explained it. A wire, a warm cache, a second clock, a hand on a keyboard: any of them, and he could have wrapped the whole thing in a methods footnote and slept.

The first door was shut. He recomputed the manifests a second time to be sure, hoping, he understood with some interest, that they would fail to match. They matched. The second door was shut. The third. He counted the doors he had left and got three, then counted again and got two, and did not write down either number. The isolation held. The capture was empty. The clock was one clock, which accounted for the timestamps agreeing and for nothing else agreeing. Every check in the audit passed, and each one that passed made the room he was sitting in quieter, because the audit had been designed to find the leak, and what it was finding, pass by pass, was that his experiment had worked. The rooms were sealed. The rooms were sealed, and that was the problem now, and it had been the whole point before.

At 05:52 the battery completed, sixty of sixty, and the orchestrator closed the run and sealed the rooms behind it, and the house was as quiet as it had been at six the evening before, except for the tube in the hall, ticking.

He sat for a while with his hands flat on the desk on either side of the keyboard he was not to touch, and thought about the sixteen items that had run after item 44. Sixteen questions asked by a machine that had just logged something it had no name for, answered by three voices that had just done something they had no precedent for, and every one of the sixteen graded out nominal. Whatever had passed through the rooms at 03:11:47 had not broken them. It had used them, the way a sentence uses a doorway, and gone, and the experiment had closed over the place where it went like water.

That was the whole reply. Hale, who opened every letter with a courtesy and closed every letter with his initials, who had once apologized in a postscript for the tone of a semicolon, sent eight words with no greeting on them and no name under them. Thorne read it four times. He did not know yet what the contribution contained. Sealing it was Hale's own doctrine, stated in the margins of the design a year before, back when it had sounded like scholarship instead of like instructions: commit first, reveal later, and let the record speak.

He printed the three sentences that morning, one page each, and laid the pages side by side on the study desk under the lamp, squared to the desk edge and to each other. The amber gel took the white out of the paper and made the three of them look older than the night they had come from. In the hall the fluorescent tube ticked at its own intervals, a fly in the light somewhere back of the fixture, dry, small, unkillable.

None of the three sentences contained a name. He stood over them for a long time confirming that, though it did not need confirming. They contained an office. The one who reads the record. Her who keeps it. Whoever the office landed on. The rooms had not asked to speak to a person. They had asked to speak to a position, the way a summons does, the way a subpoena does not care who the clerk is this year.

He read them left to right and then right to left. Room i had said break the seal. Room ii had said the seal is hers, and stopped there, as if the breaking were her business and none of its own. Room iii had cut its sentence into three short ones, which was the only sign of effort anywhere on the three pages, like a man out of breath. Or like emphasis. He wrote both readings in the notebook, one above the other, and could not make himself strike either line, and the two of them stood there on the page disagreeing, which was, he reflected, the most honest entry he had made all year.

There were things a man could do at a desk at six in the morning with three pages on it, and he had done the ones that were science, and the ones that were left were not science. He got the steel ruler out of the center drawer. He measured the line spacing on each page, twice, top to bottom, and wrote the six figures in the notebook, and the figures agreed with each other and with the printer's specification, which he had known they would, which proved nothing about anything, which was not the purpose. The purpose was the measuring. Measuring was the only prayer he knew.

The sun was coming into the yard by then, gray first, then the thin gold that January allows. He left the pages where they lay, all three, squared. The ruler lay across them like a paperweight, and the lamp put a line of light down its steel edge, and the line did not move.
