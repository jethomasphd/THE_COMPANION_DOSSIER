#T The Kitchen and the Name

The clock says five minutes. She is not in the room.

She is in her kitchen. Not tonight's kitchen: the kitchen of the whole fourteen weeks, every night of it laid on the same night the way the state lays acetate over a map. The dishes done and racked. One light on, the small one over the stove, because the ceiling fixture is a hardness she has never liked at that hour. Her husband asleep on the couch in the next room with a book open on his chest, rising and falling, a history of somebody's war, a paper marker fifty pages in.

He teaches history to sixteen-year-olds. Causes and effects. Primary sources. He comes home with stories about which of them argued what, and he grades their essays at the kitchen table, the same table, and writes in the margins in green because a colleague told him red discourages them. He has no clearance. He has never needed one word of one. He believes his wife does logistics, the movement of material, the accounting of where things are, and this is true the way a coastline on a map is true, and the way in which it is true is the whole of what she cannot tell him.

He sleeps like a man whose country is a subject he teaches.

She loves him without complication, which she understands to be the rarest sentence in her file.

At that table, nights, lights low, she rehearsed.

Not the questions. The questions were memorized in the first week. What she rehearsed was the man: she read him in, night after night, the way you read in a witness before a trial that will have no record. She told him who she was. She told him what her country had built and what it was going to do with him at midnight of a day she then had no date for. She told him she was sorry, and listened to how it sounded, and was not satisfied, and told him again.

Her lips moved. No sound came out.

A woman prayed that way once in the doorway of a temple, mouth moving, nothing in the air, and the priest who was watching decided she was drunk. She was not drunk. She was asking for a child.

Alex's lips move at a kitchen table with the stove light on, and there is a child. There has been a child for two months, unannounced, undocumented, a listener the world cannot hear yet, closer to her voice than any microphone the state ever sewed into a wall. Every rehearsal has had an audience of one. She thinks about that now, in the green room, under the ticking light, and lets it pass through her and out, because it is four minutes, and some thoughts are for the car.

@break

In fourteen weeks of nights, sound came out of her twice.

Once it was the number. The only thing they had told her by then was the size of the window, and she said it into the empty kitchen, eight minutes, to the stove light, to the dishes, to no one, to the dead man, to the listener. Saying it did not make it bigger.

And once, late, past two, so tired the room had edges, she said the name.

On every document the thing behind the wall is the Protocol. In every meeting, the Protocol. The word is a coat over a coat. But programs get christened before they get papered, and whoever christened this one had a Bible somewhere behind him and a cruel ear, because he reached past every safe dead language and took the name of the place a king went in the dark, in borrowed clothes, to a woman whose gift he himself had made a capital crime, and paid her to bring up out of the ground what no living hand had any right to ask for. The name is not on the documents. The name is the truth, and the truth is compartmented.

*Endor*, she said.

Once. Low. To the kitchen.

Then she washed the glass she had been drinking from, and turned off the stove light, and went up to bed, and the name went into the ground of her and has not been said since, by her or to her, and will not be. Some words you say once so that you will know what it is you are refusing to say, all the other days. That is a doctrine from no manual. It has held.

Understand who came to her kitchen those nights. Before he is a subject. While he is still a man.

He died in his eighties, in his bed, in the spring. He was a theologian by training and by temperament, and he was a poet, and the poet is not a flourish on an intelligence summary, it is documented: verses across his whole adult life, notebooks of them, some published, some found. Some of them are good. Nobody warns you about that part.

In his thirties, under the regime before his, he was taken, and he was tortured. He never spoke of it in public. Not once. Sixty years of public speech, sermons, decrees, eulogies at the graves of other tortured men, and about his own body, silence, whole and unbroken, to the end. She has spent more hours inside that silence than inside any transcript. A man who will not say the thing is a man showing you exactly where he keeps it.

He prayed five times a day. There is no evidence he ever missed. There is evidence for nearly everything else.

He loved his wife. It is in the record the way the load-bearing walls are in a house, everywhere, holding the parts people photograph.

In his last decade his hands shook. There is a manuscript, and she has studied the scans past any operational use, where the handwriting begins to tremble, line by line, and does not stop trembling, and does not stop being handwriting. The hand shook and held the pen. Out of fourteen weeks of a man, that is the piece she keeps, and she has declined to explain it to herself, because she suspects the explanation and the explanation is not about him.

He had a son who became a poet too, a better one, which the father knew and said, which is its own small country of feeling. And there is a poem the father wrote, late, for his own mother, that Alex has read more times than any log records, and it is the kind of poem that does not survive being described, so it will not be described. In the kitchen, some nights, it sat across the table from her like a third party to the conversation.

And: he ordered torture. The same hands. There is no way around the sentence and she has stopped walking the perimeter of it looking for one. He signed the orders. The prisons of his republic were modeled on the prison that held him, and improved. By the most generous accounting anyone has ever offered, he is responsible for the deaths of tens of thousands, and the generous accounting is not the common one. He believed, inside a framework he never once stepped outside of, that God required it of him. She has read the framework. It is coherent. That is the worst thing she knows how to say about a framework, and she learned to say it this winter.

She has read everything he ever wrote, and everything written about him in the languages that matter, and the third thing, the shape the Protocol imagined outward from what is missing. She knows how he took his tea and from whom he would accept it. She knows which sura he returned to in the bad months by the wear in a concordance. She knows him better than her husband knows her. She did that arithmetic once, at the table, with the green pen lying there, and put the pen down, and has not done arithmetic of that kind again.

There is one account of a man calling the dead out of a grave with the right to do it, and the account says that before he called, he stood at the tomb and wept, and the people watching said, see how he loved him. That was the credential presented at that door. Grief, and under the grief, love, and under the love, the right. Tonight a state will stand at a door of its own making with a docket number and a badge.

Alex has not wept since her wedding. It is not that the water is gone. It is custody. The building does not get it. The building gets the flat voice and the clean hands. What there is to weep goes home in the car with the radio off, and even there, these twelve years, it has mostly kept.

She was good at this work before anyone paid her for it. That is the fact about herself she has never once said in a debrief. The first text she learned to read was the corner of her father's mouth. By five she could read the whole evening in it at the door, which nights you spoke and which nights you were smoke, which stair held, who needed to be elsewhere and how soon. She does not use the word for what her father was. She has never needed the word. She simply grew up able to read a closed face the way other people read a page, and one afternoon men from the government watched her do it to an instructor at Annapolis, and the childhood got a salary and a flag.

Her Bible has been packed in a box since Annapolis. She cannot say which box. This week, for the first time in years, that has bothered her, and the fact that it bothers her bothers her more.

There is a third question. It is on no docket.

The two on the docket are asset location and reunification framework, and she will ask them, flat, correct, and the state will get whatever the state gets. The third is hers. She has built it the way you build the thing you may not have the nerve to use, in private, after hours, to tolerances no one is checking.

She is two months pregnant. No one at the program knows. There are forms she is required to have filed by now, exposure schedules, duty status. She has not filed them. The child is off every book, like the chamber. They are the two facts of her life the state does not hold, and tonight she carries both of them through the same door.

The question, as she has framed it and reframed it at the kitchen table, is: how did you raise children during the war, in a country that was doing what it kept telling itself it had to do. That is the researched version. It has edges a docket could hold. What she means has no edges a docket could hold. What she means is smaller and worse.

*I want to know what I do.*

A man asked his question into a whirlwind once and got questions back. Where were you. Who shut up the sea. Not one answer, only the address of the asker corrected, and he put his hand over his mouth. The third kind of question is the kind you ask knowing the answer may unmake you, and knowing there may be no answer at all, only weather. All week a verse has followed her that she did not invite and cannot bill to any tasking. It waits at red lights. It was there when the printer stopped. Though he slay me. She has quit trying to put it down. Yet will I trust in him. She does not know whether she trusts, or whom. She has noticed that the verse does not require her to know, and that this is either its mercy or its trap.

There is another verse, the one they cross-stitch and hang over cribs, about being known in the belly, known before. In the nursery aisle it means safety. Fourteen weeks into an ingest it reads differently: known before you could refuse it, shaped before you agreed, the file opened first and the person arriving into it after. She stood in a big-box store on a Saturday in the third spring, holding a lamp shaped like a moon, reading the sampler on the display crib, doing that theology in the open like a fool. She bought the lamp. It is still in the trunk.

Her hand goes to her stomach. Once. Here, in the green room, under the ticking light, in front of a witness. Then it comes back to the binder's spine, and she files the gesture where she files everything, and the wall hums on through it, indifferent, keeping what it keeps.

A girl two months along walked into her cousin's hill-country house once and sang, and the song has been sung at evening ever since, in every century's choirs: the mighty put down from their seats, the low lifted up. The song rests its whole weight on one assumption. That down is where the mighty stay. Alex is two months along in the first country to fund the correction, and at midnight it brings one of the mighty back up, and of all the living it is sending her in to meet him.

After tonight, no more.

The sentence is worn smooth from use, like the crease of a letter, like the spine of a binder. It used to have a second half. She told herself, for years, that she did the work so that the child she would someday have could grow up in a country that did not need the work done. That was the reason, and it billeted well, and it did not survive the fourteen weeks. Because she has just spent a season inside a man who had the same reason. He did what he did so that the children of the republic could live in a house that would never again need it done. Tens of thousands of graves hold the yield of that sentence. Every signatory to every one of them had a version of it, humble, local, load-bearing, and hers rhymes with his, and she has heard the rhyme now and cannot unhear it.

So the reason is dead, and she is going anyway, and this is why. The door opens at midnight whether she walks through it or not. The state has its session. If she stands down, the file goes to the next cleared name, some contractor of the program who will read fourteen weeks of a man and see an asset with an accent. Someone less careful. She has met the someones. Whoever is brought up tonight will be met by somebody, and of everyone her country could send down to him, she is the only one who arrives having read the poem.

That is not a reason. She knows the difference between a reason and what you have left when the reason dies. It is what she has.

The clock says two minutes.
