#T The Wife in Marfa

She drove because flying would have meant a manifest, and because seven hours of highway was the only kind of unrecorded she had left. West of the last Austin overpass the war thinned out into its rural dialect: a fuel sign with more digits lit than the frame was built for, a National Guard convoy heading the other way under tarps the color of dust, flatbed after flatbed, and then nothing, a hundred miles of nothing, the country flattening from hills into argument and from argument into statement. Past the Pecos the radio lost its last opinion. She let it go and drove the rest of the way inside a silence the size of the sky.

Marfa announced itself the way it always has, with light. Late afternoon, and the light came in level and gold and took everything it touched at its word: the water tower, the courthouse dome, the tin roofs burning quietly along the grid of dirt streets. It was the kind of light that separates a thing into what it is, adding nothing. She noticed herself thinking that, and noted the noticing, and let it stand. A season ago she would have crossed it out.

The house was south of the tracks, a low adobe the color of the road, with a swamp cooler ticking on the roof and a girl's bicycle leaned against the porch post, front wheel cocked, kickstand ignored on principle. In the yard a pair of live oaks, smaller and thirstier than their cousins back east, kept two circles of shade like held parking spots.

El Thorne came out through the screen door before Miranda had shut the car door, which meant she had been watched down the whole length of the street, which was fair.

"You made good time," El said. That was the greeting, all of it, and it was somehow complete. She was a tall woman gone lean rather than thin, dark hair pinned up off her neck against the heat, a potter's forearms, a schoolteacher's way of standing square to what approached. She looked at Miranda for a moment with frank assessment, the way you look at weather coming in off the plain. "You'll want water before anything. Everyone gets out of the car thinking they want answers and what they want is water."

The kitchen was cool and dim and smelled of cumin and clay slip. A window over the sink framed the mountains going blue. At the table, bent over a spiral pad with a pencil, sat the daughter, nine, barefoot, one heel hooked on the chair rung. She looked up once when they came in, filed Miranda under something, and went back to the pad. She was drawing a map. Miranda could see coastline from across the room, too much coastline to be anywhere, bays inside bays, an island labeled in careful block letters she couldn't read at that distance. The hand was steady and the hand was familiar: she had catalogued it months ago, four men at a table, signed in crayon, framed on a study wall three hundred miles east. The line had grown two springs steadier. It was the same line.

El put down glasses of water, then iced tea, then, unasked, a plate of tortillas and beans, establishing by increments that this was a household and not an interview.

"Ask your questions," El said, sitting. "I'll tell you when they're the wrong ones."

Miranda had driven seven hours with a list and found she didn't want the list. "How are you filed?" she asked instead. "Legally, I mean. I have to describe the estate's standing." It was true, and it was also the wrong first question, and she knew it, and El looked almost pleased, as if the wrongness were honest at least.

"There's no word for me," El said, without heat. "Widow of a man not proven dead. Wife of a deposit. The county has a form for everything else. Missing is a status for children and hikers. After six months they offer you a presumption, and you can take it or not take it, and either way you sign something that isn't true." She turned her glass a quarter turn on the table. "I teach eighth grade now. Two blocks from here. It has the advantage of being a thing that is what it is."

"You didn't stay in the house."

"No." The word had a whole winter in it. "That house was an instrument by the end. Every room aimed at something. You've worked in it, you know. You do not raise a child inside an instrument." She said it evenly, a decision long since amortized. "People take that to mean I was afraid of it. I wasn't afraid of it. I was its wife's landlord. There's a difference, and he'd be the first to draw you the diagram."

They talked through the cooling of the afternoon, and El gave her the marriage's version, which no document in the deposit held and which Miranda did not write down, having promised her hands she wouldn't.

"Everyone asks me one question," El said. "They dress it up but it's one question. Did he believe he was talking to the dead. And I watch them need it to be yes, because yes is a story, yes is a diagnosis, yes files him." She shook her head, once. "No. Jacob did not believe the dead speak. I'd have known. You can't share a bathroom sink with a man for eleven years and miss a belief that size. What he believed was worse, because it was about us. He believed the living had stopped listening. To the dead, to books, to each other, to anything that doesn't shout. He watched it happen inside a decade. He used to say the Flood doesn't drown you, it teaches you to breathe it." Her jaw set slightly. "The estate was never a séance. It was a listening. He called it a quiet loud enough to carry. That's the whole theology, and he'd wince to hear me use that word, and he isn't here to wince, so."

She looked out the window at the blue coming up the mountains.

"He said once, the last winter, that war doesn't change the map of what sustains a life. It reveals it. He was looking at her homework when he said it." A small motion of her chin toward the table, the pencil, the impossible coastline. "That was the map, as far as he was concerned. Whatever the record ends up saying he was doing, put that in it."

"I will," Miranda said, and El studied her a second and nodded, accepting the tender as legal.

"You'll notice I'm not asking you anything," El said. "Whether you've found. What you think. I did my asking in the spring, to every agency with a phone number, and I heard every kind of nothing there is. I'm through asking people who don't keep records. You keep them. That's better. That's the first better thing to sit in that chair."

The light went long. El stood to fill the kettle, and Miranda gathered her legal pad and squared it, the gesture that ends visits, and it was then, into the ordinary noise of water and chairs, that the daughter spoke for the first time since the filing glance.

She did not look up from the map. She finished the letter she was blocking in, set the pencil down parallel with the pad's edge, and looked at Miranda with the total practical calm of a child who has had six months to engineer one question down to its load-bearing frame.

"Is Dad in the computer?"

The kettle went quiet against the burner. El did not turn around and did not intervene, which told Miranda whose question it was and how long it had been owned.

Miranda put down the legal pad. There were kind lies available, whole shelves of them, and cruel accuracies, which were nearer to hand. What she had instead was her trade, so she answered from it, level, as she would state a finding.

"He is in the record. The record is real."

The girl held her eyes for a moment, checking the finding against instruments of her own. Then she nodded, once, the way her father's notebook closed an entry.

"So keep it."

She picked up the pencil and went back to her coastline.

At the door, in the last gold, El said, "There's a thing I have to give you. I've been deciding about you all afternoon. He'd laugh at that. He left it with the machines, so it was never mine to decide, and I decided anyway."

She went into the bedroom and came back with a single envelope. Plain, letter-sized, its corners gone soft in the way of paper that has been carried and carried and not read: six months riding in a purse, a glovebox, a coat. On the front, in the careful narrow hand Miranda knew now better than her own, one line: *for whoever the estate chooses.*

"It was on the server rack," El said. "Propped against the drives, where he knew the first person to inventory the closet would find it. Except I got there before the county did, and I took it. It's the one thing I took." She said this last part deliberately, into the record, a woman correcting a ledger. "I didn't open it. It isn't addressed to me. Eleven years, and his last piece of mail isn't addressed to me, and the strange thing, and you can file this too, is that I understand. It isn't addressed to him either, if that helps you. He was past names by then. He'd gone over entirely to roles."

She held it out.

"I'd say I hope you're the right one," El said. "But he didn't work in hope. He worked in provisions."

Miranda took the envelope to the porch rail, where the light was. The flap had been sealed with tape over the gum, belt and suspenders, a builder's habit. She opened it with her pocket knife the way she opened accessions, one clean cut, no tearing. Inside was a single page, folded once, typed on the Selectric she had catalogued in the study, its crooked lowercase t like a signature the machine gave whether you wanted one or not.

She read it standing, with the mountains going out and the first cold coming off the ground, and El on the step below her, not reading over her shoulder, looking where the sun had been.
