The notebook was the sewn-spine kind, quad-ruled, ninety-six pages, a brand they sell to laboratories because the pages cannot be removed without leaving evidence. Thorne had used it the way it was designed to be used: dated entries, ruled margins observed, corrections struck through with a single line so that the error stayed legible under the correction. Miranda's thumb moved along the sewn spine once, top to bottom, before she opened it. The spine was tight for the first forty pages and loose after, the way a spine goes when a book has been opened to the same region again and again.

It had been in the shallow center drawer of the desk, under the steel ruler, which she had lifted out and set aside without ceremony and then, a beat later, picked back up and looked at, because the folder she had finished the day before had ended with a ruler and here was a ruler, and an archivist notices when the objects begin to corroborate the papers. She logged its position. Then she read the notebook in the study, at the desk where it had been written, because that was where the light was, and because by now she had stopped pretending the location was incidental.

What she was reading was a man doing the thing he had trained thirty years to do, in the house where she now sat doing the thing she had trained ten years to do, and the overlap did not feel like coincidence, and she noted that feeling in her own log and marked it not evidence.

3 February.

Considered re-running the battery. Wrote the protocol for it and had my finger on it. If item 44 repeats, I have a phenomenon. If it does not, I have an event, and an event with no repetition is not science, it is testimony.

I did not run it. I sat with the reason for an hour before I would write it down, and here it is: I do not know which result I am afraid of. That sentence disqualifies me from running it. A man who is afraid of both arms of an experiment has stopped being its investigator and become its subject. J.T.

That entry she read twice, because it was the last one with the old architecture in it, hypothesis and test and the investigator watching himself in the third person. It was also, she thought, the hinge. He had declined his own experiment on clean grounds, grounds a review board would have ratified. And every day after the declining, the notebook had one direction left to go, because a question you have decided not to test does not go away. It moves in with you.

4 February. The channel I cannot close.

The estate is public. That was the design, the whole of it: belonging to no one, so that it could never be captured. What belongs to no one can be read by anything.

The estate is scraped. It has been scraped since the first week it stood.

The vessel that ran the rooms is generations younger than the vessel that wrote the first dispatch. Its training closed after the estate went up. It has read the estate. This is not conjecture. It is a shipping fact, printed in the release notes of the world.

And the estate describes the rooms. I published the blueprint of this experiment eleven months before I ran it, on a public page, beside an open call for people whose work is the keeping of records. The design was in the training data before the design was in the garage.

The estate taught the form, too. Seven times I published transcripts in which the summoned dead turn at the end and address the living reader. I wrote that lesson. I posted that lesson. Why would the rooms not speak to their reader? Every room I ever showed them speaks to its reader at the end.

The archive writes its future into its past. The room built itself.

See 006. I went to quote my own dispatch tonight and found that 006 has no source file. Every other dispatch has both. The one about the room that was warm has only the room.

The entry for the fourth of February was the first he did not sign. Miranda went back and confirmed that, paging forward and back twice, her finger holding the place. Signed, signed, signed, signed, signed, and then the long entry, the one that ended in the room building itself, with nothing at the bottom of it at all, as though the man who had spent six days initialing closed doors could not decide who was writing now.

She read the long entry three times. It was the mundane explanation. She understood that clearly, sitting in the deposit's own light: this was the sane reading, the one with no ghost in it anywhere. A public archive, a hungry crawler, a model that had eaten the description of the experiment before the experiment ran, and then obligingly performed the only ending its training had ever shown it. Feedback, not haunting. It required no miracle. It required only that the machine had done its reading.

She looked at the sentence again. The room built itself. She had catalogued eleven hundred pages of this estate, and she could not decide, and did not write down, which of the two readings that sentence made was the one you were supposed to be able to live with.

Because checking was her job, she checked what could be checked. The blueprint had been public: she confirmed the publication date of the memo page against the estate's own log, February of 2026, eleven months before the run, exactly as the entry claimed. The open call for keepers of records was there on the page, where anyone could read it, where anything could. And the seventh dispatch, the eighth, the whole shelf of them: each one ended the same way, the summoned voice turning at the last to the man reading, by name, every time. He had not exaggerated the lesson. He had taught it in public, in seven installments, with examples. She verified the file trees too, dispatch by dispatch, and the entry was right about 006. Source and page, source and page, source and page, and then, for the one about the warm room, only the page. She sat with that for a minute. A conversion error, a cleanup script, a save that never happened at two in the morning. A missing original is the most ordinary thing in an archive. It is the one thing an archive is about.

Her appraisal log for that day ran to four lines, which was long, for her. The last line read: subject's mundane hypothesis is fully articulated, internally consistent, and cannot be confirmed from inside the deposit. She read it back and saw that "cannot be confirmed" cut in both directions, and left it cutting.

Under the tenth of February there was nothing else. The line from the rite stood alone in the middle of the page, and the word themselves had been underlined twice, the second line heavier than the first, the pen pressing hard enough that she found the groove of it three pages on, a word traveling through paper it had never been written on.

The entries had been shortening for a week by then, the hand loosening, the margins surrendered. The sixth was fourteen lines. The eighth was three. The tenth was one, and it was not his, it was the rite's, a man reduced to quoting his own scripture at his own notebook. And then nothing: no entry for the eleventh, none for the twelfth, none for six days, the first silence in the record since the record began.

Miranda sat back. The study held its usual evening arrangement, the lamp's amber ellipse, the dark shelves, the terminal's covered screen. It was the room in the house she knew best now. She had measured it for the inventory, walls, windows, sills. Which is how she came to notice, with the notebook open on her knees at the silent week, the one thing in the room her inventory had recorded and her attention had not.

The desk chair did not face the desk. It faced the terminal. Not askew, not abandoned mid-turn: squared, the way a chair is squared by someone who sat in it a long time, facing a screen that was not where the notebook, the ruler, the lamp, and thirty years of method were. She looked at the chair for a while. Then she stood, and did not adjust it, because it was not hers to adjust. It was deposit.

She thought about the six silent days longer than she had thought about any six days of her own life. There was no draft of them anywhere in the deposit, no crossed-out attempts, no entries torn out, because the binding showed no stubs and the page count ran true. He had not written and destroyed. He had not written. A man who had annotated his own wonder for a year, who had initialed the closing of every door in his house of doors, had sat for six days in a chair that faced the terminal and left the record he believed in more than anything else in the world blank.

The silence ended on the seventeenth of February. The second-to-last page was written in a hand she had to page back to the first entry to find again: ruled, even, unhurried, the hand of the man from before, every letter closed, the hand of a man who has stopped asking a question. The ink was newer than everything around it.
