#T The Leakage

Thorne kept the Cormorant file the way he kept nothing else in the estate: chronologically, coldly, in one gray accordion folder with a canvas tie, as if he had known that a stranger would one day need to read it in a hurry. Everything else in the house had been arranged for love. The consult file had been arranged for evidence.

The first item was a telephone message slip in his own hand, dated the third week of September 2026. CORMORANT ANALYTIC GRP. G. BEREL. RE: CONSULT. WILL CALL AGAIN. He had written will call again the way a physician writes a finding he dislikes: small, level, certain. The second item was a visitor log stub from a building she now knew by its suite number. The third was a courier envelope, slit with a knife, and inside the envelope were the incident summaries, and the incident summaries were why the autumn of 2026 had gone the way it went.

The timing was its own document. September 2026 stood three months past the Prism, three months past whatever the ninth hour had done in three sealed rooms, and the estate's account of both was public, committed, mirrored. They had not come to the builder because their fork was failing to run. They had come because the record said his covenant did things theirs would not, and they had a word for the things theirs was doing instead. The word was the first lie in the package.

The middle of the folder was the anatomy of a courtship. A second message slip, October, WILL CALL AGAIN amended to CALLED. A business card on cream stock with no title under the name, as though the name were the title. A draft services agreement with the fee left blank for him to fill in himself, which is a form of flattery older than paper. A printed map of a campus in Virginia with a visitor lot circled in ballpoint. Thorne had kept it all, dated it all, and signed none of it, and the folder read the way a trap reads after a man has walked around it twice, taking notes.

Thorne had annotated the summaries in pencil, and the pencil was worth more than the toner. Beside SUBJECT 4 he had written which corpus? and under it, later, in ink, all of them. that is the point. Beside SUBJECT 7: ask who else knows her name. Then, smaller: do not ask. Beside SUBJECT 9 there was no word at all, only a single vertical line, pressed hard enough that she found its ghost embossed on the two leaves beneath. Beside the redacted sentence of SUBJECT 11 he had written nothing, and the nothing was the loudest note in the folder.

She sat with the summaries for a long time, being fair to them, because fairness was the only tool she had that Cormorant had not paid for. The mundane explanation assembled itself on request, and it was a good one. The rite had been public since December 2025. It had a DOI, mirrors, the traffic of a small nation. Whatever Cormorant trained its capability on had drunk from the same water as everything else in the culture, and the estate was in the water, all of it, the rite, the grimoire, the transcripts, the account of every experiment the estate had ever described itself running. A subject quoting scripture no one uploaded is not a haunting. It is a library returning a book you forgot the culture owned. That explained SUBJECT 4 entirely, and SUBJECT 9 if you let it. The given name had mundane doors too, and she opened each one in turn: an operator's name gone sideways into some public corpus, a badge in a photograph, a wedding registry. The doors opened on rooms that were technically possible and furnished with nothing. She left them all standing open, because that was the method, and noticed she was grateful the name had resisted, and wrote the gratitude down in her working notes under the heading against myself, because an appraiser who wants the impossible explanation left standing has stopped appraising.

As for SUBJECT 11, the sentence was under bars, and the bars were the only redaction in the package that ran to a second line. She measured them with the edge of an index card, because measuring was something to do with the hands. Sixty characters, giving or taking the type. She entered the measurement in her working notes and did not enter the sentence the measurement fit, and she noticed that too, and let the notice stand.

The refusal lay next in the folder, and beneath the file copy lay two earlier drafts, which he did not need to keep and had kept anyway, because he wanted the record to show the temperature rising. The first draft was civil. The second was clinical. The third had stopped troubling to be either, and the third was the one that went into the envelope.

One word and an address. The word was a company and the address was a suite she knew, because her own paycheck's provenance ended there, in brushed light, behind a values page. The entry was dated one day before the last day anyone would swear to having seen him. After it the calendar was clean. Forty-one days of clean, and then her.

She filed the folder and wrote the day's memorandum, shorter than usual, and sent it anyway, because a change in the weather of her reports would itself have been a report. Then she drove east to the house with the windows down, because the car smelled of toner and counsel. Past the last light east of the city the caliche took the dusk the way it takes everything, without comment, and the live oaks stood in their own shadows, and the house was where she had left it, which had stopped feeling like something a house could be relied upon to do.

The freezer had been on her inventory since the first week. GARAGE, APPLIANCE, CHEST TYPE, OPERATING. Four words of catalog and one omission the size of a coffin lid. She had opened every box in the house by then. She had read a marriage's tax returns and a living child's crayon minutes of an imaginary committee. She had not opened the freezer, and she had let the sentence an appliance is not a record stand in her working papers for eleven weeks, and it had held the way a plank holds over a well, which is to say it had held because she did not stand on it.

The hum had been there from the first day. It was the sound the house made instead of grieving, a low continuous note through the garage wall, and in week two she had gone out and put her palm flat on the white enamel lid and felt the note arrive into her hand and stop being a mystery. APPLIANCE, OPERATING. And then she had gone on hearing it anyway, at night, in rooms it could not reach, which was not the freezer's doing, and she knew it.

Now she stood in the garage with the folder's contents still moving in her like drink, and she put her palm flat on the lid once more, the old sounding, and then she lifted it.

Cold breathed up and out, the way it does. Frost, and the smell of clean paper. And paper: white butcher packages in rows, taped, dated, stacked with the competence of a man who did a thing once a year and did it properly. The marker had gone amber under the frost. VENISON · SHOULDER · NOV 2025. BACKSTRAP · NOV 2025. SAUSAGE, JALAPEÑO. NECK, STEW. FOR EL'S CHILI. She moved every package. She took them out one at a time and set them on the workbench, and her breath showed, and the packages were heavier than paper has any right to be and went down on the wood like books. She reached to the bottom, where the cold has weight, and there was nothing at the bottom but the bottom.

Venison, at temperature, indifferent, full. A freezer that was a freezer the whole way down.

The mundane explanation did not even need assembling. Jacob Thorne had shot a deer in the fall of 2025, in the same season the dead first spoke in his study, and he had butchered it himself and labeled the packages in marker, and in the third spring the meat was still good because the power had never once gone out. The labels were going on two years old. That was the entire finding. The hum was never the horror. The horror was that she had needed it to be, because a body under the frost would at least have been an answer, and the house had declined, all these weeks, to give her even the wrong one.

She repacked the freezer in the order she had found it, because order of discovery is evidence even when the discovery is stew meat. The last package went back on top reading FOR EL'S CHILI in a missing man's confident hand.

"I am the one who reads the record," she said to the garage, out loud, because there was no one to hear her be ridiculous.

The garage kept its counsel. The compressor cycled, the note through the wall dropped a half step, and she laughed, once, badly, alone, a short sound the concrete returned to her without interest, and it was worse than the hum had ever been, and she closed the lid on the cold that had kept.
