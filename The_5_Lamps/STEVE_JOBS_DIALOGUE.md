## STEVE'S DESIGN READING

I see three things:

### 1. The Interface Principle

The iPhone didn't succeed because it had features. It succeeded because it removed everything between the human and the information. No stylus. No keyboard. Just glass and your finger.

The Five Lamps needs to follow the same principle. When a student is standing in a hallway at 2 a.m. with their throat closed, the interface must be *invisible*. They open it. They type or speak their dilemma in plain language — one sentence, the way you'd tell a friend. The lamps respond. The friction is zero.

What Jacob has built with the cinematic intro — the typewriter, the scroll reveal, the portraits — that's for the *first encounter.* That's the cathedral. You walk through it once, and it changes how you understand the tool. But after that, you want a door straight to the ward.

For the product: you need two modes.
- **Cathedral mode**: The full cinematic experience. For onboarding, for faculty demonstrations, for seminars.
- **Ward mode**: Skip the intro. Straight to the five lamps. One input box. Five voices. Discharge note. Done.

### 2. The Unit of Use

The unit of use is not a "session." It's a **dilemma.** One dilemma, five lamps, one discharge note. That's the atom.

If you get the atom right, everything scales:
- Clerkship debriefs: one atom per session, 5–12 minutes.
- Ethics labs: compare atoms. How did your reasoning change?
- Portfolio of conscience: a student collects their discharge notes across clerkship. That's professional identity formation in documented form.
- Faculty review: the discharge note is auditable. The reasoning is visible. The institution can see what its students are wrestling with.

### 3. The Emotional Architecture

Here's what most people building AI tools for medicine get wrong: they think the value is in the *information.* It's not. Medical students have access to more information than any generation in history. They're drowning in it.

The value is in the *permission.* Permission to slow down. Permission to feel the weight of the dilemma. Permission to disagree with the hierarchy — at least in the privacy of their own deliberation.

The Five Lamps gives permission in the form of five voices that are *more authoritative than the attending.* Hippocrates outranks everyone in that hallway. When a student hears Jung say "What part of you is making this decision? Is it the healer, or the one who is afraid?" — that's not information. That's liberation.

The emotional architecture of this product is: **You are not alone in this. The greatest physicians in history stood here too, and they disagreed with each other. Your confusion is not weakness. It is the beginning of ethical reasoning.**
